Clean Water Future: Modular STEM Series (Extended) - Texas TRIO Edition

A comprehensive ten-module STEM curriculum designed for High School TRIO (Upward Bound/UBMS) students in the North Texas region. Adapted for 40-minute instructional blocks plus one field experience, this series guides students from understanding physical filtration to chemical analysis, industrial observation, and engineering design. Ideally suited for Saturday Academies or Summer Bridge programs to fulfill rigorous math/science curriculum requirements.

Notes & Observations

Curriculum Narrative Document: Clean Water Future: Modular STEM Series (Extended) - Texas TRIO Edition

Executive Summary

The 'Clean Water Future' modular series is a rigorous, interdisciplinary STEM curriculum specifically designed for High School TRIO programs (Upward Bound and UBMS) in the North Texas region. Over ten intensive modules, students transition from fundamental physical science concepts to complex chemical analysis, industrial engineering observation, and digital 3D design. This curriculum aims to close the opportunity gap for first-generation, low-income students by providing hands-on laboratory experience, applied mathematics, and direct exposure to high-demand careers in environmental science and engineering. By framing water scarcity and purification through the lens of civil and chemical engineering, the program prepares participants for the academic demands of postsecondary STEM degrees while addressing real-world infrastructure challenges relevant to their local communities.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is meticulously aligned with the Higher Education Act (HEA) Section 402C, meeting the federal mandates for the Upward Bound and Upward Bound Math-Science programs. The activities satisfy the 'required services' for 'instruction in laboratory science' (34 CFR 645.11(b)(1)) and 'instruction in mathematics' (34 CFR 645.11(b)(2)) by integrating rigorous lab protocols with algebraic concentration calculations. Career exposure modules comply with the requirement for 'career awareness' and 'exposure to STEM careers' under 34 CFR 645.11(b)(6). Furthermore, all associated costs for consumable lab supplies and educational technology are justified as 'allowable and necessary' under EDGAR/2 CFR 200.453 (Materials and supplies), and the field trip component is structured as an educational exception to entertainment costs pursuant to 2 CFR 200.438 and 34 CFR 645.40, as it provides unique industrial exposure unavailable in a traditional classroom setting.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is mapped directly to the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for high school science, specifically targeting Environmental Systems (SCI.ENV.5.B, 9.C) and Chemistry (SCI.CHEM.10.A, 10.B, 10.D) regarding water filtration, mixtures, and solutions. It also incorporates Career and Technical Education (CTE) standards for Engineering (CTE.ENG.3.A, 5.A). Furthermore, the series adheres to the Texas STEM Education Framework, prioritizing Indicator 1.C (Inquiry-Based Learning), 3.A (STEM Design Process), and 6.A (Career Exploration). This alignment ensures that the program reinforces public school academic achievement while providing the 'rigorous' curriculum required for TRIO program performance reports.

Field Expeditions

The curriculum offers high-impact field experiences within the Dallas-Fort Worth Metroplex, specifically targeting sites within 60 miles of Dallas, Tarrant, and Johnson counties to maximize instructional time. Primary options include the Tarrant Regional Water District (TRWD) and the Fort Worth Rolling Hills Water Treatment Plant, where students observe the industrial scaling of sedimentation and flocculation. Additionally, the Trinity River Audubon Center in Dallas provides an ecological context for wetland filtration and environmental monitoring. These sites offer students a unique window into the professional infrastructure of North Texas, connecting classroom experiments to massive civil engineering works and municipal management careers.

Module Narratives

Module 1: The Physics of Dirty Water (Theory & Prep)

Students engage in a foundational inquiry into the properties of mixtures and solutions, establishing a baseline for the physics of water contamination. By creating standardized 'synthetic' contaminants and utilizing turbidity charts for measurement, students learn the importance of control variables and scientific rigor. This activity focuses on defining the problem through observation, a critical step in the engineering design process.

Module 2: The Filtration Engineer (Construction)

In this hands-on engineering lab, students apply civil engineering principles to design and fabricate a multi-stage bio-sand filter. By manipulating granular media of varying porosity‚Äîincluding activated charcoal, sand, and gravel‚Äîstudents explore how material science and layering logic impact physical separation efficiency, bridging the gap between theory and functional prototype construction.

Module 3: Testing & Flocculation (Analysis)

Students transition into a performance testing phase, where they calculate flow rates and quantify the effectiveness of their filters. The introduction of chemical flocculants (Alum) demonstrates advanced chemical engineering concepts, allowing students to observe the molecular-level clumping of contaminants and the subsequent impact on water clarity.

Module 4: Chemical Analysis & Safety

Focusing on laboratory professionalism, this module introduces students to high-level safety protocols and analytical chemistry. Students utilize chemical indicators to measure pH, alkalinity, and specific contaminants in 'mystery' samples, learning how to interpret complex colorimetric data and relate it to municipal water safety standards.

Module 5: Thermal Separation (Distillation Lab)

Students explore thermodynamics and phase changes through the construction and operation of a distillation apparatus. This activity challenges students to move beyond physical filtration to understand the energy-intensive process of thermal separation, highlighting the scientific differences between treating 'grey water' versus desalinating brine.

Module 6: Verification & Concentration (Math)

This module emphasizes the 'Math' in UBMS by requiring students to perform post-lab calculations on purification efficiency. Students utilize algebraic formulas to determine molarity and parts-per-million (PPM), engaging in critical thinking to compare the cost-benefit analysis of filtration versus distillation from an economic and energy perspective.

Module 7: Industrial Scale & Careers

Students pivot from bench-top experiments to systems engineering, analyzing flow diagrams of large-scale municipal treatment plants. By exploring the differences between batch and continuous processing, students identify various STEM career pathways, including Plant Operators, Civil Engineers, and Quality Assurance Managers.

Module 8: Field Experience - Site Visit

The field experience provides a professional immersion where students observe industrial-scale infrastructure in the North Texas region. By interacting with working engineers and witnessing the massive filtration basins of plants in Tarrant or Dallas County, students validate their classroom learning through the lens of public service and large-scale infrastructure management.

Module 9: CAD Basics (Digital Design)

Transitioning into the digital domain, students learn the fundamentals of Computer-Aided Design (CAD). This module introduces spatial reasoning and geometric manipulation as students create 3D models of filter components, preparing them for modern engineering workflows where digital prototyping precedes physical manufacturing.

Module 10: Digital Prototype Review (Engineering)

In this culminating activity, students participate in a formal 'Design Review' process, a staple of professional engineering. They must present their digital prototypes and provide technical justifications for their design choices, synthesizing their knowledge of physics, chemistry, and manufacturing to defend their solutions against potential failure points.
