STEM Architect Master Guide

Cultivating the Future: High School Agriculture & Horticulture STEM Pathway

This comprehensive curriculum is designed for TRIO Upward Bound (UB) and Upward Bound Math-Science (UBMS) programs to guide high school students through the principles of modern agriculture and horticulture. It consists of 20 modular activities that integrate Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) with federal grant objectives. The curriculum covers soil science, hydroponic engineering, precision agriculture, genetics, coding, fluid dynamics, and career pathways, providing a holistic view of the 'Farm to Future' pipeline.

Target: High School (Ages 14-18) • Total Time: 20 sessions of 40 minutes (approx. 13-14 hours total contact time) • Est. Cost: $282.00

Soil Detectives: The Chemistry of Growth

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: Both

Students act as soil scientists to analyze soil samples for pH, Nitrogen, Phosphorus, and Potassium (N-P-K) levels. They will determine which crops are best suited for specific soil profiles and calculate necessary amendments.

Learning Objectives

  • Analyze soil composition and chemical properties.
  • Understand the role of macronutrients in plant health.
  • Apply scientific methods to interpret data and make agricultural recommendations.

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: Both

Students explore sustainable horticulture by designing and building a small-scale Kratky (passive) hydroponic system. They will learn about water conservation and engineering design constraints.

Learning Objectives

Curriculum Narrative: Cultivating the Future - High School Agriculture & Horticulture STEM Pathway

Executive Summary

Cultivating the Future is a comprehensive, multi-disciplinary STEM curriculum designed for Upward Bound (UB) and Upward Bound Math-Science (UBMS) programs. The curriculum comprises 20 rigorous modular activities that bridge the gap between traditional agricultural practices and modern technological innovation. By integrating principles of soil chemistry, genetic engineering, autonomous systems, and fluid dynamics, the program prepares high school students for the 'Farm to Future' pipeline. This pathway is specifically engineered to improve academic performance in core laboratory sciences and mathematics while exposing first-generation, low-income students to high-growth career sectors in Ag-Tech, environmental engineering, and food science.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is meticulously designed to comply with the Higher Education Act (HEA) of 1965, specifically Section 402C(b) regarding the mandatory provision of instruction in mathematics and laboratory science for Upward Bound projects. Per 34 CFR Part 645.11(a)(1), the activities meet federal requirements for providing academic instruction in subjects necessary for success in education beyond high school. Furthermore, the curriculum supports the UBMS-specific mandate under 34 CFR 645.11(b) for a rigorous secondary school program of study. All associated materials and technology costs are aligned with EDGAR/2 CFR 200.453 cost principles for materials and supplies, ensuring that expenditures are necessary and reasonable for the performance of the federal award, particularly for instruction in laboratory science and computer science (Sec. 402E(b)).

Standards Alignment

The modules are fully aligned with the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for Career and Technical Education (CTE) and Science. Key alignments include Agriculture, Food, and Natural Resources (Chapter 130), Biology (112.34), Chemistry (112.35), and Physics (112.39). The curriculum also adheres to the Texas STEM Framework by emphasizing the Engineering Design Process (EDP), computational thinking, and the application of college-level mathematical modeling to solve real-world agricultural challenges, such as crop yield optimization and structural engineering of vertical farms.

Field Expeditions

Educational value is extended through strategic field trips within 60 miles of Dallas, Tarrant, and Johnson counties. Students can explore University-level research at the Texas A&M AgriLife Center in Dallas, focusing on urban forestry and hydroponics. The Botanical Research Institute of Texas (BRIT) in Fort Worth offers exposure to molecular genetics and conservation careers. To reinforce soil science and plant physiology, the Harvest Project at The Dallas Arboretum provides a living laboratory for edible garden science. Finally, Cleburne State Park offers a localized context for environmental geology and soil erosion studies, allowing students to observe the real-world impact of the conservation strategies practiced in the classroom.

Module Narratives

Soil Detectives: The Chemistry of Growth

Students engage in high-level diagnostic chemistry by analyzing soil N-P-K and pH levels. This activity emphasizes scientific inquiry and chemical properties, requiring students to interpret data to provide professional agricultural recommendations for crop health.

Hydro-Engineering: Designing Passive Systems

Focusing on sustainable horticulture, students utilize the engineering design process to construct Kratky hydroponic systems. This module introduces students to urban agricultural careers and resource conservation, emphasizing root respiration and fluid delivery.

Precision Ag: Intro to GIS and Remote Sensing

Students leverage simulated geospatial data to analyze field health via NDVI color mapping. This activity bridges technology and agriculture, teaching students how to use remote sensing to optimize variable rate irrigation and reduce environmental waste.

The Business of Botany: Crop Yield Math

This rigorous math-focused module requires students to apply algebraic formulas to calculate agricultural profit margins and risk. Students must navigate real-world economic variables like drought and market shifts, reinforcing financial literacy and statistical reasoning.

Ag-Careers: Beyond the Tractor

This career exploration module deconstructs agricultural myths by highlighting high-tech roles such as drone pilots and food scientists. Students engage in professional networking simulations and research university degree plans to visualize their path to postsecondary success.

Mendel in the Garden: Probability and Genetics

Students apply Mendelian laws of inheritance to predict crop phenotypes using Punnett squares. The activity integrates biology with probability math, demonstrating how selective breeding and genetic traits are foundational to modern food security.

Coding the Harvest: Micro-Controllers in Ag

Focusing on the Internet of Things (IoT), students program microcontrollers to automate soil monitoring. This module introduces computer science logic and hardware interfacing, preparing students for careers in agricultural automation and systems development.

Solar Scouting: Agrivoltaics Physics

By exploring the intersection of renewable energy and farming, students investigate the physics of light and shadow. This activity uses geometric modeling to optimize dual-use land systems, highlighting sustainable engineering and thermodynamics.

The pH of Preservation: Food Science

Students act as quality control technicians, using chemical indicators to determine the safety profiles of food. This module emphasizes microbiology and chemistry, specifically focusing on how pH levels inhibit pathogenic growth in the food supply chain.

Berry DNA Blueprint: Biotech Basics

In this hands-on biotechnology lab, students extract genomic DNA from plant tissue. This activity teaches precise laboratory techniques and provides a gateway to discussing the ethical and scientific implications of genetic modification in global agriculture.

Vertical Farm Architects: Structural Engineering

Students tackle urban density challenges by designing vertical farm models that must withstand load-bearing constraints. This engineering challenge emphasizes structural integrity, space efficiency, and the physics of weight distribution.

Erosion Engineers: Soil Conservation

This environmental science module tasks students with mitigating land degradation through engineered defenses. Students observe geological processes in a controlled environment, learning how civil engineering can protect vital topsoil and water quality.

Aquaponic Synergy: The Nitrogen Cycle

Focusing on symbiotic ecosystems, students analyze the chemical conversion of ammonia to nitrate. This advanced biology module emphasizes systems thinking and water chemistry, illustrating the interdependence of zoology and horticulture.

Logistics of Lunch: Supply Chain Math

Students use graph theory to solve the 'Traveling Salesman Problem' within the context of food distribution. This activity focuses on optimization, mathematical networking, and reducing the carbon footprint of the global supply chain.

Integrated Pest Detective: Entomology Lab

Utilizing microscopy and dichotomous keys, students classify insects to design non-chemical pest management plans. This activity reinforces biological taxonomy and ecological balance, focusing on reducing chemical dependency in food production.

Fluid Dynamics in Irrigation: Physics of Flow

This physics module explores Bernoulli's principle by challenging students to design gravity-fed irrigation. Students calculate pressure and flow rates, applying hydraulics and algebra to solve complex distribution problems in farm engineering.

Drone Flight Pathing: Geometry in the Sky

Students apply geometric modeling to plan autonomous UAV flights. By calculating field area and swath width, they learn to optimize flight algorithms for maximum battery efficiency and total coverage, a critical skill in modern ag-tech.

Compost Thermodynamics: The Heat is On

Students investigate exothermic reactions and microbial respiration by building bioreactors. This module applies thermochemical equations to organic waste management, highlighting the role of microbiology in soil health and sustainability.

Fungal Frontiers: Mycology Lab

Focusing on the Kingdom Fungi, students practice aseptic laboratory techniques to inoculate substrates. This activity explores the role of decomposers in nutrient cycling and introduces students to specialty crop production and mycology.

Capstone: The Martian Greenhouse

The curriculum culminates in a high-stakes design challenge where students synthesize knowledge of astrobiology and engineering. Teams must design a food system for an off-world colony, presenting a technical proposal that accounts for extreme environmental constraints.
