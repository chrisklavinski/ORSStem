STEM Architect Master Guide

Future Ready: Introduction to Artificial Intelligence for Texas High Schoolers

A comprehensive 20-module curriculum designed for TRIO Upward Bound and UBMS programs in Texas. This curriculum breaks down AI fundamentals, ethics, coding concepts, and career planning into standalone 30-minute lessons. It moves from basic definitions to technical mechanics, creative applications, and finally career readiness.

Target: High School (Ages 14-18) • Total Time: 10 Hours (20 Individual Modules x 30 minutes) • Est. Cost: $55.00

Module 1: The Hierarchy of Intelligence (AI vs. ML vs. DL)

Duration: 30 minutes TRIO: Both

An introductory lesson defining the relationship between Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning using concentric circle visualization.

Learning Objectives

  • Define Artificial Intelligence as the umbrella term for smart machines.
  • Differentiate between Machine Learning (learning from data) and Deep Learning (neural networks).
  • Classify common apps (Netflix, Siri) into the AI hierarchy.

Materials

  • Whiteboard and Markers$0 (Standard Classroom)
  • Printed Handouts: 'The AI Nesting Dolls' diagram$5.00 (Printing)

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services

Citations
HEA Sec. 402C(b) - Academic instruction in mathematics and science, 34 CFR 645.11(a)(1) - Academic instruction in core subjects, 2 CFR 200.453 - Materials and supplies costs.

Teacher Guide

1.

Step 1: Introduction (5 mins) - Start with 'Is a calculator AI?' (No). 'Is Alexa AI?' (Yes).

2.

Step 2: The Hierarchy (10 mins) - Draw circles. AI (Outer), ML (Middle), DL (Inner).

3.

Step 3: Analogy (5 mins) - Use the 'Vehicle' analogy. AI is transportation, ML is the engine, Data is gas.

4.

Step 4: Check for Understanding (10 mins) - Sticky note activity sorting terms like Self-driving car vs Excel.

Curriculum Narrative: Future Ready: Introduction to Artificial Intelligence for Texas High Schoolers

Executive Summary

Future Ready: Introduction to Artificial Intelligence is a comprehensive 20-module curriculum specifically designed for Federal TRIO Upward Bound (UB) and Upward Bound Math-Science (UBMS) programs serving Texas high schoolers. This 10-hour course demystifies the rapidly evolving landscape of AI through 30-minute standalone lessons that bridge the gap between abstract computer science theory and real-world career application. Moving from foundational concepts like machine learning hierarchies and algorithmic logic to complex ethical considerations and generative modeling, the curriculum culminates in a design-thinking capstone project. By integrating hands-on kinesthetic activities with rigorous academic content, this program prepares first-generation and low-income students for post-secondary success in high-demand STEM fields, ensuring they are competitive in a global economy increasingly driven by automated systems.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is meticulously aligned with the Higher Education Act (HEA) of 1965, specifically Section 402C(b), which mandates that UB projects provide academic instruction in mathematics, laboratory science, and English composition. By utilizing data science for mathematical modeling and natural language processing (NLP) for linguistic analysis, the program fulfills the requirements for 'instruction in core subjects' as outlined in 34 CFR 645.11(a)(1) and 34 CFR 645.11(b)(1) for UBMS. Furthermore, the inclusion of career exploration and college tours aligns with 34 CFR 645.11(a)(3). All associated instructional supplies, software access, and field trip expenditures are structured to meet the cost principles of EDGAR and 2 CFR 200, specifically 2 CFR 200.453 (Materials and supplies costs) and 2 CFR 200.467, ensuring all costs are necessary, reasonable, and allocable to the performance of the TRIO grant.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is rigorously aligned with the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for Computer Science I (¬ß126.33), STEM (¬ß130.402), and Career Preparation I (¬ß127.14). Specific focus is placed on TEKS 126.33 (c)(4)(A) regarding computational thinking and 130.402 (c)(2)(D) regarding the history and evolution of technology. Additionally, the modules incorporate Texas STEM Framework standards across all four Sub-Chapters: Sub-Chapter A (Inquiry and Design), Sub-Chapter B (Critical Thinking), Sub-Chapter C (Technology Operations), and Sub-Chapter D (Career Development), providing a robust interdisciplinary framework that meets Texas Education Agency (TEA) expectations for college and career readiness.

Field Expeditions

Educational enhancement is facilitated through five strategic field trip options within 60 miles of Dallas, Tarrant, and Johnson counties. These sites include the Perot Museum of Nature and Science in Dallas to explore the 'Robot Arena,' the University of Texas at Arlington (UTA) Research Institute for exposure to high-level automation labs, and the Richardson IQ (Innovation Quarter) to witness machine learning in the startup ecosystem. Additionally, visits to the Fort Worth Museum of Science and History and Dallas College (Richland Campus) provide students with direct exposure to the Texas energy sector and post-secondary degree pathways in Computer Science. These trips serve to transition theoretical classroom knowledge into tangible career visualization, meeting the TRIO mandate for exposure to collegiate and professional environments.

Module Narratives

Module 1: The Hierarchy of Intelligence (AI vs. ML vs. DL)

Students begin by demystifying 'The Algorithm' through a visual and conceptual exploration of Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning. This session uses concentric circle visualization to help students classify everyday technologies like Siri and Netflix, establishing a rigorous academic foundation for defining smart systems.

Module 2: A Brief History of Minds (Turing to Transformers)

This module provides historical context by tracing the evolution of AI from Alan Turing's 'Imitation Game' to the modern era of Generative AI. Students analyze the periods of 'AI Winter' to understand how technological hype and investment cycles influence scientific progress.

Module 3: Algorithms (The Recipe for Intelligence)

In this high-engagement session, students write 'code' for a teacher to perform a simple task like making a sandwich. The activity highlights the necessity of precision in algorithmic thinking and introduces students to flowcharting, a critical skill for both software engineering and logistics careers.

Module 4: Data - The Fuel for AI

This lesson explores the distinction between structured and unstructured data, teaching students how machines consume information. By attempting to manually 'tag' unstructured photo data, students gain an appreciation for the scale and complexity of the datasets required for modern machine learning.

Module 5: The Neural Network Game (Kinesthetic Learning)

By roleplaying as individual neurons in a physical network, students experience the mechanics of forward passes and backpropagation. This kinesthetic activity makes the abstract mathematical thresholds and weights of neural networks tangible and easier to grasp.

Module 6: How LLMs Think (Next Token Prediction)

Students de-mystify Large Language Models by participating in a 'Next Token' prediction game. This module helps students understand that AI 'thinking' is actually probabilistic math, which provides a technical explanation for common AI 'hallucinations'.

Module 7: Prompt Engineering 101 (The RTFC Framework)

This session introduces the RTFC (Role, Task, Format, Constraint) framework, teaching students how to control AI outputs. This skill is framed as a modern career competency, allowing students to use AI as a sophisticated study and research assistant.

Module 8: Computer Vision (How Machines 'See')

Students investigate how digital images are represented as grids of numbers (pixels) and RGB values. This module connects computer science to physics and optics, showing how self-driving cars use matrix math to interpret the physical world.

Module 9: Supervised vs. Unsupervised Learning

Through a card-sorting simulation, students differentiate between learning with labeled data (Supervised) and finding patterns in raw data (Unsupervised). This addresses core statistical concepts and explains how algorithms power recommendation engines like Spotify.

Module 10: Generative Art (Diffusion Models)

This module explores the intersection of art and science by explaining Diffusion models‚Äîthe process of turning 'noise' into images. Students engage in creative prompting while debating the ethical and copyright implications of AI-generated content.

Module 11: Algorithmic Bias (Garbage In, Garbage Out)

Focusing on social responsibility, this module uses case studies like 'Gender Shades' to show how human bias is inherited by AI. Students act as 'Ethics Auditors' to identify missing demographics in datasets and propose fairer training methods.

Module 12: Deepfakes & Media Literacy

Students learn to identify synthetic media and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). This session provides essential civic engagement training, teaching students how to verify information in an era of digital misinformation.

Module 13: AI in Healthcare (Diagnosis Detective)

By simulating the role of a radiologist, students compare their own accuracy in detecting pneumonia on X-rays with the speed of AI. This module highlights the concept of 'Human-in-the-loop' and explores career paths in biomedical engineering.

Module 14: NLP & Sentiment Analysis

This module bridges English Language Arts and Computer Science by teaching students how computers quantify emotion in text. Students calculate sentiment scores for sentences, learning how brands monitor social media for customer satisfaction.

Module 15: Robotics vs. AI (Body vs. Brain)

Students distinguish between the hardware of robotics and the software of AI. By designing a hypothetical robot, they learn about sensors (inputs) and actuators (outputs), which are fundamental concepts in mechatronics and systems engineering.

Module 16: Future of Work (Automation vs. Augmentation)

Using O*NET research tools, students investigate how AI will change their chosen dream careers. This counseling-heavy module helps students identify 'future-proof' skills that rely on human empathy, complex problem-solving, and creativity.

Module 17: Ethics - The Trolley Problem 2.0

Students engage in a philosophical debate centered on self-driving car programming. By grappling with 'The Trolley Problem,' students learn that engineering decisions often involve complex ethical tradeoffs that cannot be solved by code alone.

Module 18: Capstone Prep - Identifying a Problem

Students begin their final project by applying Design Thinking to their own communities. They brainstorm local problems‚Äîranging from traffic to recycling‚Äîand determine if these issues can be mitigated using data-driven AI solutions.

Module 19: Capstone Project - Paper Prototyping

Moving from idea to design, students create low-fidelity paper prototypes of their AI applications. They map the user journey and annotate their sketches to show exactly where the AI processing happens, simulating the software development lifecycle.

Module 20: Pitch Perfect (Presenting AI Ideas)

In the final session, students deliver a 1-minute elevator pitch for their AI solution. This workshop focuses on employability skills, teaching students how to translate technical jargon into a compelling value proposition for non-technical audiences.
