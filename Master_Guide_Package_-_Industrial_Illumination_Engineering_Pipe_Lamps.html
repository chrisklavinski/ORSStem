Industrial Illumination: Engineering Pipe Lamps

A comprehensive 6-lesson project-based learning module where High School students explore electrical physics, structural engineering, and industrial design by constructing functional lamps using PVC and Black Iron piping. Students will progress from low-cost prototyping with PVC to creating finished industrial-style steel lamps, utilizing materials standard in construction and trade industries. This curriculum integrates Texas STEM standards with federal TRIO objectives.

Target: High School (Ages 14-18) • Total Time: 240 minutes (6 sessions, 40 minutes each) • Est. Cost: $102.75

Lesson 1: The Physics of Light & Electrical Safety

Duration: 40 minutes TRIO: Both

Students learn the fundamentals of AC electricity, circuit safety, and how to use a multimeter before touching construction materials. This establishes the safety baseline for the course.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand basic AC current flow and the function of hot, neutral, and ground wires.
  • Demonstrate proper safety protocols for handling electrical components.
  • Identify the components of a standard lamp socket assembly.

Show students how to set the multimeter to the Continuity (beep) setting to test for broken wires.

Lesson 3: PVC Prototype Construction

Duration: 40 minutes TRIO: UBMS

Students build their lamp structures using Schedule 40 PVC. This allows for low-cost errors and learning threading/fitting mechanics before moving to expensive steel.

Learning Objectives

  • Measure and cut PVC pipe to specific design lengths.
  • Assemble a stable mechanical structure.
  • Understand tolerance and friction fits.

Materials

  • 1/2 inch Schedule 40 PVC Pipe (10ft length)$6.00 per length
  • PVC Fittings (Elbows, Tees, Adapters)$0.50 - $1.50 each
  • PVC Cutters$12.00 each (Program Equipment)
  • Measuring Tape$5.00 each (Program Equipment)

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Mentoring, Exposure to STEM Programs
Citations
HEA Sec. 402E(b) - Hands-on experience in laboratories, 34 CFR 645.11(b)(1) - Personal counseling (Mentoring during build), 34 CFR 645.11(a)(2) - Science instruction, 2 CFR 200.453 - Materials and supplies

Teacher Guide

1.

Show how to ratchet the PVC cutters slowly to get a straight cut; do not force it.

2.

Explain that we are 'dry fitting' (no glue) because we reuse these parts for the next class, but real plumbing requires solvent welding.

3.

Discuss the Center of Gravity: if the lamp head extends too far forward, the base must be heavy or wide enough to counter it.

4.

Maintain strict supervision at the cutting station to ensure fingers remain clear of blades.

4

Lesson 4: Wiring the PVC Lamp

Duration: 40 minutes TRIO: Both

Students install the electrical components into their PVC prototype. They will pull wire through the tubing and wire the socket. This bridges the structural build with the electrical theory.

Learning Objectives

  • Successfully fish electrical wire through a tubular conduit.
  • Wire a polarized plug and socket correctly (Hot to Brass, Neutral to Silver).
  • Tie an Underwriter's Knot for strain relief.

Materials

  • Lamp Cord sets (18 gauge)$5.00 each
  • Make-a-Lamp Socket Kits$6.00 each
  • Electrical Tape$2.00 roll

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in Lab Science, Technical Skills Training
Citations
HEA Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in science, 34 CFR 645.11(a)(2) - Science instruction, 2 CFR 200.453 - Materials and supplies

Teacher Guide

1.

Demonstrate the 'Underwriter's Knot' which prevents the wire from being ripped out of the socket terminals if the cord is yanked.

2.

Show close-ups of the wire insulation: Ribbed/Squared is Neutral, Smooth/Round is Hot.

3.

Explain that the wide blade on a plug is Neutral, connecting to the silver screw, ensuring the threaded socket shell is never electrified.

4.

Physically inspect every student's connections before allowing them to close the socket shell.

5

Lesson 5: Industrial Steel Lamp Build

Duration: 40 minutes TRIO: UB

Students graduate to Black Iron Pipe. This involves handling heavier materials, cleaning industrial grease, and dealing with thread friction. This lesson simulates real-world trade work.

Learning Objectives

  • Assemble threaded steel pipe with proper torque.
  • Clean and prep industrial materials (degreasing).
  • Compare material properties of PVC vs. Steel (density, rigidity).

Materials

  • 1/2 inch Black Iron Pipe Nipples (various lengths)$2.00 - $6.00 each
  • Black Iron Fittings (Elbows, Flanges)$2.50 - $6.00 each
  • Degreaser / Shop Rags$10.00 per class
  • Work Gloves$3.00 pair

Teacher Guide

1.

Warn students that black iron pipes come coated in protective oil that stains clothes. Gloves are mandatory.

2.

Discuss 'Galvanic Corrosion' (Science connection) - why we don't mix copper and steel directly without a dielectric union usually.

Explain that pipe threads are tapered (NPT), meaning they get tighter as you turn them, creating a mechanical seal.

4.

Demonstrate how to thoroughly degrease the pipe before assembly so the lamp is clean for home use.

  • Perform quality assurance (QA) testing.
  • Present a cost-benefit analysis of materials used.

Curriculum Narrative: Industrial Illumination: Engineering Pipe Lamps

Executive Summary

Industrial Illumination: Engineering Pipe Lamps is a rigorous six-session project-based learning module designed for high school students to master the intersection of electrical physics, structural engineering, and industrial design. By progressing from low-fidelity PVC prototyping to the construction of professional-grade black iron steel lamps, students gain hands-on experience with trade-standard materials and diagnostic tools like multimeters. The curriculum emphasizes career readiness, financial literacy through Bill of Materials (BOM) management, and the application of Ohm's Law, culminating in a functional product that demonstrates mastery of circuit topology and material science.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is specifically designed to meet the mandatory service requirements for Upward Bound (UB) and Upward Bound Math-Science (UBMS) programs as outlined in the Higher Education Act (HEA) Sections 402C(b) and 402E(b). It provides direct instruction in laboratory science and mathematics, satisfying 34 CFR 645.11(a)(2) and (b)(1). All costs associated with the consumables (PVC, steel pipe, wiring kits) and non-consumable equipment (multimeters, cutters) are allowable under 2 CFR 200.453 (Materials and supplies) and 2 CFR 200.313 (Equipment), provided the items are used to support the academic objectives of the TRIO project. Furthermore, the inclusion of financial literacy and career exposure aligns with 34 CFR 645.11(b)(5-6) and EDGAR cost principles regarding reasonable and necessary expenditures for academic enrichment.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is aligned with several Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) standards, including Physics (¬ß112.39.c.5.F) for circuit calculations, Engineering Design and Presentation I (¬ß130.362.c.5.A) for orthographic sketching, and Scientific Research and Design (¬ß130.372.c.2.H) for data communication. Within the Texas STEM Framework, the project emphasizes the Engineering Design Process and STEM Content Application, challenging students to utilize mathematical models to solve real-world structural and electrical problems while adhering to rigorous safety and manufacturing protocols.

Field Expeditions

To reinforce classroom learning, students will participate in two strategic field trips within 60 miles of Dallas, Tarrant, and Johnson counties. The first visit to Tarrant County College (South Campus) Center of Excellence for Energy Technology provides exposure to large-scale industrial pipe-fitting and commercial wiring, highlighting collegiate pathways in HVAC and Electrical Technology. The second trip to the Perot Museum of Nature and Science in Dallas allows students to explore the Texas Instruments Engineering and Innovation Hall, where they can interact with exhibits on circuit logic and structural integrity, bridging the gap between their desktop projects and global engineering achievements.

Module Narratives

Lesson 1: The Physics of Light & Electrical Safety

Students engage with the fundamental physics of AC electricity, utilizing digital multimeters to verify continuity and learning the critical distinctions between hot, neutral, and ground conductors. This session establishes a professional safety baseline, requiring students to demonstrate 100% accuracy in circuit labeling and safety assessments before handling construction materials.

Lesson 2: Design, BOM, and Budgeting

This module introduces engineering drafting and fiscal responsibility as students create orthographic sketches and a comprehensive Bill of Materials (BOM). By navigating real-world supplier databases to source components within a $30 constraint, students apply mathematical modeling to balance aesthetic design with economic reality.

Lesson 3: PVC Prototype Construction

Students transition to physical fabrication using PVC to explore structural mechanics and the center of gravity. This hands-on activity allows for rapid iterative design and low-cost error correction, teaching students the importance of dry-fitting and mechanical tolerances before moving to final metal assembly.

Lesson 4: Wiring the PVC Lamp

Rigor increases as students integrate electrical systems into their prototypes, mastering the Underwriter‚Äôs Knot for strain relief and ensuring proper polarization for consumer safety. This stage bridges the gap between theoretical physics and functional engineering, requiring high-level fine motor skills and attention to sequence.

Lesson 5: Industrial Steel Lamp Build

Moving to industrial materials, students handle black iron steel, learning about NPT thread tapering, degreasing processes, and the material properties that differentiate steel from polymer counterparts. This session simulates a real-world trade environment, focusing on torque, material processing, and the prevention of oxidation.

Lesson 6: Final Wiring, Testing, and Analysis

The curriculum concludes with quality assurance (QA) testing and a formal presentation where students perform a cost-benefit analysis. Students must defend their design choices, compare material efficiencies, and communicate their engineering process, demonstrating the practical application of their technical and academic skills.
