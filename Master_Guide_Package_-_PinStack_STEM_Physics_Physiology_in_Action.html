PinStack STEM: Physics & Physiology in Action

A rigorous, federal grant-compliant STEM curriculum designed for Upward Bound (UB) and UB Math & Science (UBMS) programs. This field curriculum utilizes the controlled environments of PinStack facilities (Bowling Lanes, Laser Tag Arena, High Ropes Course) as active physics and biology laboratories. The curriculum is structured to justify costs under 2 CFR 200 by transforming recreational equipment into scientific apparatus for measuring momentum, friction, infrared signal processing, and physiological stress responses. It aligns directly with Texas TEKS and federal objectives to improve standardized test performance in Math and Science.

Rolling with Physics: Momentum & Friction on the Lanes

Duration: 1.5 Hours TRIO: Both

Students transform bowling lanes into Newtonian physics labs. They calculate average velocity, estimate the coefficient of kinetic friction based on oil pattern deceleration, and calculate momentum transfers (impulse) during pin impact.

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate average velocity using distance/time intervals.
  • Analyze conservation of momentum (p=mv) during ball-pin collisions.
  • Differentiate between static and kinetic friction on oiled surfaces.
  • Calculate Kinetic Energy (KE = 1/2mv^2) of the projectile.

The Physiology of Adrenaline: Laser Tag Biometrics

Duration: 1 Hour TRIO: UBMS

Students wear heart rate monitors during Laser Tag sessions to analyze the physiological effects of acute stress (fight or flight). They also explore the physics of infrared light transmission and receiver technology.

Learning Objectives

  • Measure and graph heart rate recovery times.
  • Explain the sympathetic nervous system response ('fight or flight').
  • Understand Infrared (IR) light properties on the Electromagnetic Spectrum.
  • Calculate target heart rate zones.

Curriculum Narrative Document: PinStack STEM: Physics & Physiology in Action

Executive Summary

PinStack STEM: Physics & Physiology in Action is a rigorous, multi-disciplinary field curriculum specifically engineered for Federal TRIO Upward Bound (UB) and Upward Bound Math & Science (UBMS) programs. This curriculum transforms high-capacity recreational facilities into active scientific laboratories, utilizing bowling lanes, laser tag arenas, and high ropes courses as apparatus for empirical data collection. By bridging the gap between theoretical classroom concepts and real-world kinetic environments, the program seeks to improve student proficiency in Texas TEKS-aligned Math and Science standards while providing exposure to high-growth STEM career pathways. The curriculum is designed to meet the highest standards of academic rigor, ensuring that all off-site activities are focused on measurable learning outcomes and data analysis.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is meticulously structured to comply with the Higher Education Act (HEA) Section 402C(b), which mandates instruction in mathematics and laboratory science for UB/UBMS projects. Per 34 CFR 645.11(a) and (b), the program provides direct academic instruction in core curriculum areas required for high school graduation and postsecondary success. To satisfy federal cost principles under EDGAR and 2 CFR 200, all associated costs‚Äîincluding facility rentals and instructional supplies‚Äîare justified as 'necessary and reasonable' (2 CFR 200.403-404). The program distinguishes itself from unallowable 'entertainment' or 'social' costs by requiring a documented pedagogical framework where equipment is utilized as scientific instrumentation, and costs are allocable (2 CFR 200.405) based on the direct benefit of increasing student competency in physics, biology, and trigonometry.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is mapped directly to the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for secondary education. Core alignments include Physics 4(A), 4(D), and 6(C) regarding Newtonian mechanics and conservation of momentum; Biology 10(A) concerning physiological regulation and homeostasis; and Geometry 9(A) regarding trigonometric ratios in right triangles. Additionally, the curriculum integrates Texas STEM Standards 1(A), 2(B), 3(A), and 4(B), emphasizing the engineering design process, technical communication, and critical thinking. This alignment ensures that participating students receive supplemental instruction that reinforces the state-mandated academic foundation required for the STAAR EOC assessments and college readiness.

Field Expeditions

The instructional component takes place at PinStack facilities located in Las Colinas and Plano, Texas, situated within a 60-mile radius of Dallas, Tarrant, and Johnson counties. These venues provide unique, controlled environments that cannot be replicated in a traditional classroom setting: regulation-length bowling lanes provide the necessary distance for calculating friction coefficients of oiled surfaces; two-level laser tag arenas serve as dark-room laboratories for studying infrared electromagnetic waves; and suspended ropes courses provide the verticality required for clinometer-based triangulation and the study of mechanical advantage in pulley systems. These field experiences offer high-impact, hands-on laboratory science instruction that bridges the gap between abstract formulas and physical reality.

Module Narratives

Rolling with Physics: Momentum & Friction on the Lanes

In this module, students transform bowling lanes into Newtonian physics laboratories to conduct high-level kinetic research. Participants utilize digital scales and precision timers to calculate average velocity and the momentum transfers (p=mv) occurring during ball-pin impacts. By analyzing the deceleration of various ball masses across oiled lane surfaces, students derive the coefficient of kinetic friction, moving beyond textbook definitions to empirical observation. This activity emphasizes career relevance in sports engineering and ballistics while maintaining academic rigor through complex data recording and the calculation of Kinetic Energy (KE = 1/2mv^2).

The Physiology of Adrenaline: Laser Tag Biometrics

This activity focuses on human biology and biotechnology through the lens of acute stress responses. Students utilize wearable heart rate monitors to track their biometric data during a high-activity laser tag session, mapping the sympathetic nervous system's 'fight or flight' response. Students are tasked with graphing their recovery curves to analyze how the body returns to homeostasis post-exertion. Furthermore, the module explores the physics of the electromagnetic spectrum, as students examine how infrared (IR) sensors and emitters facilitate signal processing, providing a dual-focus on biomedical engineering and optical physics.

Vertical Engineering: Ropes Course Trigonometry

Utilizing the high ropes course as a structural case study, students apply advanced geometric and trigonometric principles to the built environment. Students construct and use clinometers to measure angles of elevation, applying the Tangent ratio to calculate the height of vertical structures without direct physical measurement. The narrative further explores mechanical engineering through the analysis of pulley-driven belay systems and the distribution of loads across triangular trusses. This module is designed to foster interest in civil and structural engineering by demonstrating the practical necessity of trigonometry in construction and safety design.
