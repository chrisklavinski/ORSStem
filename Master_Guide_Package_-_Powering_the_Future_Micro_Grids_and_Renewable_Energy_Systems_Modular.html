Powering the Future: Micro-Grids and Renewable Energy Systems (Modular)

This comprehensive hands-on curriculum utilizes a 300W/260Wh portable solar generator to teach high school students the principles of photovoltaic energy conversion, battery storage technology, electrical load calculation, and sustainable engineering design. Adjusted for standard scheduling, this curriculum is divided into eight 40-minute sessions suitable for daily classroom instruction or segmented Saturday Academy workshops. It is designed to meet strict DOE TRIO compliance for instruction in Mathematics and Laboratory Science.

Curriculum Narrative: Powering the Future: Micro-Grids and Renewable Energy Systems (Modular)

Executive Summary

This rigorous, hands-on curriculum is designed to equip high school students with the technical and mathematical skills required to navigate the emerging renewable energy economy. By utilizing a 300W/260Wh portable solar generator as a mobile laboratory, students engage in eight modular sessions covering photovoltaic physics, battery storage chemistry, electrical load analysis, and grid resilience. The curriculum moves from foundational scientific principles to advanced engineering simulations, culminating in a 'Blackout Scenario' that requires students to apply algebraic modeling and resource management to maintain critical infrastructure. This program bridges the gap between theoretical classroom science and vocational STEM application, preparing participants for post-secondary pathways in electrical engineering, environmental science, and sustainable technology.

Module Narratives

Session 1: The Physics of Photovoltaics

Students are introduced to the semiconductor physics of solar cells, exploring how photons catalyze electron flow via the photoelectric effect. This session emphasizes safety protocols for DC systems and establishes the academic rigor of the unit by differentiating between potential energy and current, providing a foundation for laboratory science.

Session 2: Solar Lab - Angle and Efficiency

In this data-centric field experiment, students measure irradiance and wattage output. By manipulating the angle of incidence and simulating environmental interference like partial shading, students apply geometric principles and record empirical data to analyze efficiency losses in renewable systems.

Session 3: The Energy Bank - AC/DC & Inverters

This session demystifies the conversion of energy, focusing on the mechanical and thermal realities of inverters. Students explore the 'heat tax' of electrical conversion and learn the critical distinction between storage capacity (Watt-hours) and instantaneous demand (Watts), mirroring real-world systems engineering.

Session 4: Load Analysis Lab

Through direct measurement of appliances, students identify 'vampire loads' and distinguish between surge and continuous power requirements. This activity builds career relevance by simulating the work of energy auditors and facilities managers, focusing on precise data collection and appliance efficiency.

Session 5: The Math of Battery Life

Students engage in high-level algebraic modeling by applying dimensional analysis to calculate system runtimes. Using the formula T = (Capacity * Efficiency) / Load, students must predict system performance, bridging the gap between theoretical math and practical engineering constraints.

Session 6: The Blackout Scenario - Budgeting

As the unit transitions into problem-based learning, students are presented with an emergency power-outage profile. They must perform a triage of electrical needs vs. wants, assigning priorities to life-safety equipment and critical communications under strict resource scarcity.

Session 7: The Blackout Scenario - Scheduling Simulation

This advanced simulation requires students to create a 24-hour time-domain model of energy usage. By plotting solar gain against hourly drain, students must optimize their usage schedule to prevent system failure, demonstrating a mastery of variable relationships and predictive modeling.

Session 8: Grid Defense & Career Pathways

The curriculum concludes with technical presentations and a synthesis of career opportunities. Teams defend their micro-grid strategies against peer critique, followed by a guided exploration of the college majors and trade certifications required to enter the multibillion-dollar renewable energy workforce.
