Retro-Future Engineering: Building a Desktop Arcade Machine

This modular STEM curriculum guides students through the engineering, programming, and construction of a desktop arcade cabinet. Designed for TRIO Upward Bound and UBMS programs, it integrates Geometry (CAD), Computer Science (Linux/OS), Physics (Circuitry), and Engineering (Assembly). The program emphasizes the 'Maker' mindset while adhering to strict federal cost principles, ensuring all hardware is justified as educational supplies for rigorous laboratory instruction in math and science.

Curriculum Narrative: Retro-Future Engineering ‚Äì Desktop Arcade Construction & Embedded Systems

Executive Summary

The Retro-Future Engineering curriculum is a 16-hour modular STEM program designed specifically for TRIO Upward Bound and UBMS students to bridge the gap between abstract academic concepts and tangible engineering applications. By constructing a functional desktop arcade machine, students engage in a high-interest project that serves as a laboratory for instruction in Geometry, Physics, Computer Science, and Mechanical Engineering. This curriculum leverages the 'Maker' mindset to foster college readiness, providing students with the technical vocabulary, precision measurement skills, and computational thinking required for success in post-secondary STEM degree plans. The program concludes with a fully realized electronic system, demonstrating the practical intersection of hardware and software.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is engineered to align strictly with the allowable services and cost principles outlined by the U.S. Department of Education for TRIO programs. Under the Higher Education Act (HEA) Section 402C and 34 CFR ¬ß 645.11, the activities provide mandatory instruction in mathematics (Geometry/Algebra) and laboratory science (Physics/Computer Science). Per the cost principles defined in EDGAR (2 CFR ¬ß 200.453), all hardware components‚Äîincluding Raspberry Pi computing devices and electronic sensors‚Äîare classified as necessary instructional supplies required for the delivery of the specialized curriculum. These materials remain the property of the grantee to be used for recurring instructional cycles, ensuring no 'gift of public funds' and maintaining compliance with federal regulations regarding the programmatic use of technology in academic tutoring and instruction.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is rigorously mapped to the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) and the Texas STEM Framework. Key alignments include Geometry standards for volume and surface area (111.43), Engineering Design standards for technical sketching and graphics (130.362, 130.365), and Physics standards for electrical circuits and voltage (112.39). Additionally, the program addresses Computer Science TEKS (126.33) through its focus on operating systems and hardware-software interaction. By integrating these standards, the program ensures that students develop 'STEM Fluency' in critical thinking, computational logic, and prototyping as defined by the Texas Education Agency‚Äôs STEM certification criteria.

Field Expeditions

To reinforce classroom instruction, the curriculum identifies three high-value field trip destinations within 60 miles of the Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex. The UTA FabLab in Arlington provides students with exposure to university-level digital fabrication and research facilities, fulfilling UBMS requirements for college campus exposure. Mouser Electronics in Mansfield offers a unique view into the global supply chain and industrial engineering logistics, connecting electronic component theory to international commerce. Finally, the Perot Museum of Nature and Science in Dallas provides interactive reinforcement of physics and robotics concepts through its Engineering and Innovation Hall, ensuring students see the real-world scale of the concepts learned in the arcade build.

Module Narratives

Design Thinking & Physical Constraints

In this foundational module, students are introduced to the engineering design process by navigating physical constraints. Using digital calipers, students perform precision measurements of electronic components to calculate internal volume requirements, transforming a physical object into a mathematical model. This activity emphasizes academic rigor in geometry and teaches students the industrial importance of 'tolerances' and 'clearance' in product design.

Introduction to CAD Software

Students transition from 2D sketches to 3D digital environments using professional-grade CAD software. This module focuses on spatial reasoning and coordinate geometry as students translate their physical measurements into a digital prototype. By mastering tools like extrusion and dimensioning, students gain career-relevant skills utilized in architecture and mechanical engineering, bridging the gap between digital design and physical manufacturing.

The Hardware-Software Handshake

This module explores the architecture of single-board computers (SBCs) and the fundamentals of operating systems. Students engage in a laboratory science activity where they flash an OS image to storage media, learning the critical distinction between file storage and bootable partitions. The session highlights career pathways in computer engineering and embedded systems, emphasizing the logic of how hardware and software communicate.

Linux Command Line Basics

Moving beyond graphical interfaces, students enter the Linux terminal to learn the syntax of command-line navigation. This module develops computational thinking and digital literacy as students use text-based commands to manage file systems and configure network settings. The rigor of this activity prepares students for advanced post-secondary studies in cybersecurity and systems administration, focusing on the precision of programming language.

Circuit Theory and Continuity

Rooted in Applied Physics, this module introduces students to the flow of electricity and the mechanics of switches. Students utilize multimeters to test for continuity, learning to identify Normally Open (NO) and Normally Closed (NC) circuits. This hands-on lab reinforces the concepts of voltage, current, and resistance, providing a practical application for algebraic variables in an electrical engineering context.

Mechanical Assembly Strategy

The final assembly phase focuses on the industrial engineering aspects of the project, including tool safety, fastening techniques, and structural integrity. Students must follow a complex technical guide to assemble the cabinet housing, requiring teamwork and spatial problem-solving. This module emphasizes the importance of manufacturing precision and quality control, connecting the previous modules of design, logic, and circuitry into a finished engineering product.
