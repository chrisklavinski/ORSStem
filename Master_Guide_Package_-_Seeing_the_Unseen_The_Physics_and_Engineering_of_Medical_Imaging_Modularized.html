Seeing the Unseen: The Physics and Engineering of Medical Imaging (Modularized)

This curriculum module immerses students in the science behind non-invasive medical imaging, adapted into six 40-minute high-impact sessions. Students will explore the electromagnetic spectrum through X-ray simulations, understand sound wave reflection via Ultrasound modeling, and apply geometry to Computed Tomography (CT) scanning logic. The unit emphasizes the intersection of physics, anatomy, and mathematics while highlighting high-growth healthcare careers. Content is aligned with Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) and structured to meet Federal TRIO (UB/UBMS) grant objectives regarding rigorous laboratory science instruction.

Target: High School (Ages 14-18) • Total Time: 4 Hours (6 Modules of 40 Minutes) • Est. Cost: $74.00

Module 1: The Electromagnetic Spectrum & X-Ray Physics

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: Both

Part 1 of the X-Ray unit. Students learn the physics of high-energy waves and prepare their 'patients' for analog simulation using photosensitive materials.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the position of X-rays on the electromagnetic spectrum.
  • Explain the relationship between frequency, wavelength, and energy.
  • Hypothesize how material density affects wave absorption.

Materials

  • Chicken bones or opaque plastic bone models$5.00
  • Various density items (metal washers, cotton balls)$0.00
  • EM Spectrum Chart/Poster$0.00

Teacher Guide

1.

1. **Theory (15 mins):** Present the Electromagnetic Spectrum. Focus on why we use X-rays for bone (high energy passes through skin, absorbed by calcium).

2.

2. **Hypothesis Generation (10 mins):** Show students the materials (metal, bone, cotton). Ask them to rank them by density and predict which will block the most light.

3.

3. **Patient Construction (15 mins):** Have groups arrange their items on a standard piece of paper (as a placeholder) to finalize their design. They must create a scenario (e.g., 'Broken Arm with Screw').

4.

4. **Storage:** Ensure designs are documented or kept safe for Module 2.

2

Module 2: Developing the Diagnosis (Analog Simulation)

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: UBMS

Part 2 of the X-Ray unit. Students expose their layouts to UV light using cyanotype paper, develop the images, and interpret the results as radiologists.

Learning Objectives

  • Demonstrate how negative images are formed in radiology.
  • Analyze a 'shadow graph' to identify foreign objects vs. biological structures.
  • Formulate a diagnostic report based on visual data.

Materials

  • Sun-sensitive Cyanotype Paper (pack of 30)$15.00
  • Clear plastic sheet protectors$5.00
  • UV Flashlights or Sunlight$12.00
  • Water trays for rinsing$2.00

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in laboratory science, Mentoring (Peer review of diagnosis)
Citations
Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in science, Sec. 402E(b) - Hands-on research experience (UBMS), 34 CFR 645.11(a)(3) - Science instruction, 34 CFR 645.43(a) - Scientific research activities (UBMS), 2 CFR 200.404 - Reasonable costs, 2 CFR 200.453 - Materials and supplies

Teacher Guide

1.

1. **Setup (5 mins):** Dim the lights. Distribute one sheet of cyanotype paper per group.

2.

2. **Transfer (5 mins):** Students quickly move their arranged objects onto the sensitive paper and cover with plastic to flatten.

3.

3. **Exposure (10-15 mins):** Move to sunlight or turn on UV lamps. Explain that the UV light is acting as the 'Radiation'.

4.

4. **Development (5 mins):** Remove objects. Rinse paper in water until the yellow chemical turns white/clear and the background turns blue.

5.

5. **Assessment (10 mins):** Students analyze the contrast. White = Bone/Metal (High Density). Dark Blue = Air/Space (Low Density).

3

Module 3: Ultrasound Physics & The Coordinate Plane

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: UBMS

Part 1 of the Ultrasound unit. Focuses on the theory of sound waves (echolocation) and the mathematical setup of 3D mapping using grid systems.

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the principle of echolocation/reflection.
  • Set up a 3-dimensional coordinate system (x, y, z).
  • Calibrate measurement tools for scientific accuracy.

Materials

  • Pre-made Gelatin Phantoms (Teacher prepared prior)$15.00
  • Bamboo skewers$2.00
  • Rulers$0.00

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in Mathematics (Geometry), Academic advice (Course selection for Engineering)
Citations
Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in mathematics, Sec. 402E(d)(1) - Intensive instruction in mathematics and science, 34 CFR 645.11(a)(3) - Math instruction, 34 CFR 645.11(b)(5) - Mentoring, 2 CFR 200.453 - Materials and supplies

Teacher Guide

1.

1. **Prep Check:** Ensure gelatin boxes are set and opaque (taped sides).

2.

2. **Theory (15 mins):** Explain that Ultrasound uses high-frequency sound. It calculates distance by Time x Speed. We are simulating this by physically probing.

3.

3. **Math Setup (15 mins):** Students must draw a 1cm x 1cm grid on the lid of their box. Label columns (A, B, C...) and rows (1, 2, 3...). This is the X/Y plane.

4.

4. **Tool Calibration (10 mins):** Students use rulers to mark their skewers (probes) every 0.5 cm to ensure accurate depth reading (Z-axis).

4

Module 4: The Blind Biopsy (Data Collection)

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: Both

Part 2 of the Ultrasound unit. Students execute the experiment, systematically probing the phantom tissue and recording raw data for analysis.

Learning Objectives

  • Collect systematic scientific data.
  • Differentiate material properties (hardness/texture) via tactile feedback.
  • Record 3-variable data points (x, y, z).

Materials

  • Gelatin Phantoms (from Module 3)$0.00
  • Calibrated Skewers$0.00
  • Data Entry Sheets / Excel$0.00

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in laboratory science, Tutorial services
Citations
Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in science, Sec. 402C(b) - Tutorial services, 34 CFR 645.11(a)(3) - Science instruction, 34 CFR 645.11(b)(1) - Academic support, 2 CFR 200.403 - Allocability of costs

Teacher Guide

1.

1. **Technique (5 mins):** Instruct students to push the skewer straight down (perpendicular). Slanted probes will ruin the data.

2.

2. **Execution (30 mins):** This is the labor-intensive phase. Students must systematically check grid points (A1, A2, A3...).

3.

3. **Differentiation:** If they hit the bottom of the box, record 'MAX'. If they hit an object, record the depth value.

4.

4. **Tactile Feedback:** Ask them to note if the object felt hard (marble) or squishy (grape).

5

Module 5: 3D Reconstruction & Analysis

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: UBMS

Part 3 of the Ultrasound unit. Students translate their raw numerical data into a visual map to identify the hidden object, then verify against reality.

Learning Objectives

  • Convert numerical data sets into visual representations (graphs/maps).
  • Analyze data to determine the size and shape of an unseen object.
  • Compare model predictions with physical reality (Error Analysis).

Materials

  • Graph Paper or Laptops with Excel$0.00
  • Colored Pencils$5.00

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in Mathematics (Graphing/Data Analysis), Exposure to STEM careers (Biomedical Engineering)
Citations
Sec. 402E(d)(1) - Intensive math instruction, Sec. 402E(b) - Research experience, 34 CFR 645.11(a)(3) - Math instruction, 34 CFR 645.43(a) - Hands-on experience, 2 CFR 200.453 - Materials and supplies

Teacher Guide

1.

1. **Visualization (10 mins):** Show how to turn numbers into a picture. Example: Depths of 0-2cm = Red, 2-4cm = Yellow, Bottom = Blue.

2.

2. **Mapping (15 mins):** Students color their grid paper based on their data. The 'shape' of the tumor/organ should appear.

3.

3. **Diagnosis (5 mins):** Based on the shape and the 'Hard/Soft' notes, what is it? (e.g., 'A 3cm round cyst').

4.

4. **Verification (10 mins):** Remove the tape/lid. Pull the gelatin out. Compare the drawing to the real object. Discuss why errors happened (probe bent, object moved).

6

Module 6: Computed Tomography (CT) Logic

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: UB

A fast-paced module where students use clay modeling to understand Tomography (slicing) and algorithmic reconstruction.

Learning Objectives

  • Define Tomography as imaging by sections.
  • Visualize spatial relationships by converting 2D cross-sections into 3D mental models.
  • Collaborate to reconstruct a hidden object from limited data.

Materials

  • Modeling Clay (Multiple colors)$10.00
  • Dental floss (for slicing)$3.00

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in Mathematics (Spatial reasoning), Educational services for economic literacy (Salary discussion)
Citations
Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in mathematics, Sec. 402C(b) - Career awareness, 34 CFR 645.11(a)(3) - Math instruction, 34 CFR 645.11(b)(1) - Career awareness, 2 CFR 200.453 - Materials and supplies

Teacher Guide

1.

1. **Intro (5 mins):** CT Scanners spin around the body, taking slices. We will simulate this.

2.

2. **Build (10 mins):** Students work in pairs. One builds a 'Mystery Organ' (clay ball with a star/shape inside of a different color).

3.

3. **Slice (10 mins):** The builder slices the model using dental floss (clean cuts).

4.

4. **Analyze (10 mins):** The builder shows the slices one by one to the partner. The partner must sketch the 3D internal shape.

5.

5. **Closing (5 mins):** Discuss how this relates to finding tumors without surgery.

Curriculum Narrative: Seeing the Unseen - The Physics and Engineering of Medical Imaging (Modularized)

Executive Summary

Seeing the Unseen is a high-impact, modularized curriculum designed to bridge the gap between abstract physical science and practical medical technology for secondary students. Spanning six 40-minute sessions, the program immerses participants in the mechanics of non-invasive diagnostics, covering the electromagnetic spectrum via X-ray simulations, wave reflection through ultrasound modeling, and spatial geometry via Computed Tomography (CT) logic. By integrating rigorous laboratory science with real-world healthcare career pathways, the curriculum prepares students for post-secondary success in STEM and Health Science disciplines, fostering critical thinking through hands-on data collection and diagnostic interpretation.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is explicitly designed to satisfy the legislative requirements for Federal TRIO Upward Bound (UB) and Upward Bound Math-Science (UBMS) programs. It fulfills the mandate for 'instruction in laboratory science' as defined in the Higher Education Act (HEA) Sec. 402C(b) and provides the 'intensive instruction in mathematics and science' required for UBMS programs under HEA Sec. 402E(d)(1). The instructional design aligns with Title 34 CFR Part 645.11, specifically addressing core academic instruction in science (a)(3) and career awareness (b)(1). All associated instructional costs, including laboratory consumables and modeling materials, are 'allocable and reasonable' under the cost principles of EDGAR 2 CFR 200.404 and 2 CFR 200.453, ensuring compliance for federal grant fund expenditures.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is meticulously aligned with the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for Physics, Biology, and Geometry. Core alignments include Physics 7(B) and 7(E), focusing on the characteristics of electromagnetic and sound waves, and Biology 2(H) regarding evidence-based communication. Mathematical rigor is maintained through Geometry 11(D), applying 3D coordinate formulas. The modules utilize the Texas STEM Framework, emphasizing Science Practices such as planning investigations and data analysis to ensure students develop the competencies required for competitive performance on state assessments and college-level coursework.

Field Expeditions

To reinforce classroom learning, this curriculum includes strategic field trip options within 60 miles of Dallas, Tarrant, and Johnson counties. A visit to the UT Southwestern Medical Center Simulation Center in Dallas provides students with direct exposure to high-fidelity diagnostic tools and clinical training environments. Alternatively, the Perot Museum of Nature and Science offers the Texas Instruments Engineering and Innovation Hall, where exhibits on wave physics and sensor technology provide tangible connections to the engineering design process. These excursions are vital for fulfilling TRIO mandates for 'exposure to academic programs and cultural events' while highlighting local high-growth career opportunities in the DFW metroplex.

Module Narratives

Module 1: The Electromagnetic Spectrum & X-Ray Physics

This introductory module establishes a rigorous foundation in wave mechanics by exploring high-energy radiation. Students investigate the relationship between frequency, wavelength, and energy to understand why X-rays are the primary tool for skeletal imaging. The activity transitions from theory to laboratory preparation as students construct 'patient' models of varying material densities, hypothesizing how different physical properties affect wave absorption and preparing them for diagnostic simulation.

Module 2: Developing the Diagnosis (Analog Simulation)

In this laboratory-intensive session, students act as radiologists by exposing their patient models to UV light using photosensitive cyanotype paper. This analog simulation provides a visible representation of how radiation creates contrast based on material density. The exercise culminates in the production of a formal 'Radiology Report,' where students must analyze their processed images to identify foreign objects and fractures, honing their skills in observation and evidence-based conclusion.

Module 3: Ultrasound Physics & The Coordinate Plane

Shifting from light to sound, this module introduces the principles of echolocation and wave reflection. Students are tasked with the mathematical setup of a three-dimensional mapping system, applying the Cartesian coordinate plane to a 'blind' medical environment. By calibrating probing tools and constructing physical grids, students integrate geometry and physics to prepare for systematic data collection in an opaque environment.

Module 4: The Blind Biopsy (Data Collection)

Focusing on the rigor of scientific data collection, this module simulates the work of a sonographer. Students perform systematic probing of 'gelatin phantoms' to record raw data points across X, Y, and Z axes. This activity emphasizes the importance of precision and tactile feedback in medical diagnostics, requiring students to differentiate between material textures without direct visual confirmation, a key skill in non-invasive surgical procedures.

Module 5: 3D Reconstruction & Analysis

This module serves as the computational core of the ultrasound unit, where students translate their raw numerical data into a visual 3D reconstruction. Utilizing heat-mapping techniques and color-coding, students generate a topographical map of the hidden objects within their phantoms. The session concludes with a 'reveal' and error analysis, where students compare their mathematical models to physical reality, fostering critical thinking regarding data integrity and software logic.

Module 6: Computed Tomography (CT) Logic

The final module explores the engineering behind Computed Tomography by simulating the process of 'slicing' 3D objects into 2D data sets. Using clay modeling and cross-sectional analysis, students collaborate to reconstruct complex internal shapes from limited visual data. This activity highlights the intersection of spatial reasoning and medical software algorithms, providing students with a clear understanding of how high-tech scanners synthesize multiple images into a comprehensive diagnostic view.
