Sky High: Applied Drone Aviation & Engineering Curriculum - A Modular STEM Framework for DOE TRIO Programs

Executive Summary

The Sky High curriculum is a specialized 30-hour STEM program designed to bridge the gap between abstract physics concepts and real-world aviation technology for high school students. Structured in 40-minute modular blocks, this course is optimized for the scheduling constraints of Upward Bound (UB) and Upward Bound Math-Science (UBMS) programs. Students progress through a rigorous sequence of instruction including Newtonian physics, FAA regulatory compliance, Python-based autonomous coding, and structural engineering. By combining hands-on flight labs with computational thinking, the curriculum prepares participants for postsecondary pathways in aerospace engineering, computer science, and unmanned systems, specifically tailored for implementation within the Dallas-Fort Worth educational ecosystem.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is designed to meet the rigorous requirements for 'Required Services' as outlined in the Higher Education Act (HEA) Section 402C(b), providing academic instruction in laboratory science, mathematics, and computer science. Per 34 CFR 645.11(a)(1), the activities offer direct instruction in reading, writing, and laboratory science, while the 'Math Olympics' and coding modules fulfill the mandate for intensive mathematics instruction required for UBMS programs under 34 CFR 645.12. All associated costs, including micro-drones, safety equipment, and software licenses, are categorized as 'Instructional Supplies' or 'Equipment' and comply with the cost principles of the Education Department General Administrative Regulations (EDGAR) and 2 CFR 200.453. Furthermore, the integration of industry-standard technology supports the 34 CFR 645.11(b)(1) objective of exposing students to high-growth career sectors.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is strictly aligned with the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for Physics (¬ß112.39), Geometry (¬ß111.41), and Career and Technical Education (¬ß130.402). Key alignments include the analysis of motion in two dimensions, the application of the Engineering Design Process (EDP), and the use of algorithms to solve complex problems. Additionally, the program incorporates the Texas STEM Framework strategies, specifically focusing on Project-Based Learning (Strategy 2) and Technology Integration (Strategy 3), ensuring that students develop the fluency skills‚Äîsuch as computational thinking and resilience‚Äînecessary for success in the Texas workforce.

Field Expeditions

The curriculum includes strategic field trip opportunities within 60 miles of Dallas, Tarrant, and Johnson counties to reinforce classroom learning through exposure to the DFW 'Aviation Corridor.' Key destinations include the Hillwood AllianceTexas Mobility Innovation Zone (MIZ) in Tarrant County, which provides a live look at autonomous logistics and commercial drone testing; the Bell Flight Experience Center in Fort Worth, focusing on vertical lift technology and urban air mobility; and the Frontiers of Flight Museum in Dallas, which offers historical context and advanced aerodynamics labs. these excursions provide critical 'real-world context' as defined by the Texas STEM Framework, connecting student learning to the regional aerospace industry.

Module Narratives

Session 1: The Physics of Flight & FAA Regulations

This introductory module establishes the academic foundation of the course by exploring Newton‚Äôs Third Law of Motion and the four forces of flight. Students engage in a laboratory environment where they calculate thrust-to-weight ratios, ensuring academic rigor through mathematical application. The session also addresses civic responsibility by preparing students for the FAA TRUST safety requirements, blending regulatory literacy with physical science.

Session 2-3: Flight Boot Camp (Manual Control)

Students transition to active flight labs, mastering the kinematics of unmanned aircraft through manual control drills. This module emphasizes spatial awareness and precision, requiring students to execute complex maneuvers like the '4-point box' while maintaining constant altitude. These sessions reinforce the physics of motion while developing the hand-eye coordination required for commercial piloting careers.

Session 4: Introduction to Drone Coding (Python/Blocks)

This session introduces computational thinking by mapping 3D coordinate geometry to autonomous flight commands. Students learn to translate mathematical concepts‚Äîsuch as transformations and rotations in a plane‚Äîinto functional code. By writing their first 'Hello World' flight scripts, students gain exposure to software engineering and the logic of closed-loop control systems.

Session 5-6: The Autonomous Maze Challenge

Serving as a mid-course practicum, this module requires students to apply geometry and measurement skills to navigate a physical obstacle course autonomously. Students must measure environmental distances, convert those units into code parameters, and iterate on their scripts through a debugging process. This activity mirrors real-world industrial automation and rewards persistence and logical troubleshooting.

Session 7: Payload Engineering - Design & Prototype

Focusing on the Engineering Design Process (EDP), this session challenges students to build physical attachments for drone delivery scenarios. Students must account for Center of Gravity (CG) and structural integrity, applying mechanical engineering principles to create lightweight, functional prototypes. This module highlights the intersection of creativity, physics, and material science.

Session 8: Payload Engineering - Testing & Data Analysis

In this data-driven lab, students conduct test flights of their prototypes to analyze the relationship between payload weight and battery efficiency. By recording flight times and observing stability thresholds, students utilize linear regression and graphing skills to find the 'critical failure point' of their designs, providing a high-level application of scientific inquiry and statistical analysis.

Session 9-10: Search & Rescue Math Olympics

The capstone module gamifies advanced mathematics and mission planning in a high-stakes Search and Rescue (SAR) simulation. Teams must use algebraic equations to solve for velocity, search grid areas, and time-on-station constraints. To succeed, students must demonstrate mastery of all previous modules‚Äîcombining flight skill, coding logic, and mathematical precision to solve real-world emergency management problems.
