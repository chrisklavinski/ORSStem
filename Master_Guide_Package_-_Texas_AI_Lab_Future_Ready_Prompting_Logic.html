STEM Architect Master Guide

Texas AI Lab: Future Ready Prompting & Logic

A 20-module immersive curriculum designed to transition high school students from passive consumers of technology to active creators using Artificial Intelligence. This course covers Large Language Model (LLM) mechanics, prompt engineering, ethics, and rigorous STEM integration. It is specifically designed to align with DOE TRIO UB/UBMS objectives by providing instruction in mathematics, laboratory science (computer science), and rigorous academic preparation as outlined in the Higher Education Act Section 402C.

Target: High School (Ages 14-18) • Total Time: 13.5 Hours (20 sessions x 40 minutes) • Est. Cost: $365.10

1. The Ghost in the Machine: What is Generative AI?

Duration: 40 minutes TRIO: Both

Introduction to Large Language Models (LLMs), how they differ from search engines, and setting up accounts with safety protocols.

Learning Objectives

  • Define Generative AI and LLMs.
  • Distinguish between a search engine query and a generative prompt.
  • Establish digital citizenship and safety rules for AI use.

Materials

  • Chromebooks or Laptops$300 per unit (amortized)
  • Access to Gemini (Google) or ChatGPT (Free Tier)$0
  • Projector/Smartboard$0 (Facility use)

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in laboratory science (Computer Science), Exposure to new technology
Citations
HEA Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in Core Curriculum, 34 CFR 645.11(b)(1)(ii) - Laboratory Science Instruction, 34 CFR 645.11(a)(1) - Academic Support, 2 CFR 200.453 - Materials and supplies costs

Teacher Guide

1.

Begin by explaining that LLMs are essentially 'predictive text on steroids' that calculate the probability of the next word.

2.

Perform a live demonstration: Search 'How does a car engine work' on Google (showing links) vs. ChatGPT (showing a synthesized explanation).

3.

Distribute and review the TRIO program's Acceptable Use Policy, specifically addressing academic integrity and plagiarism.

2. Prompt Engineering 101: The PREP Method

Duration: 40 minutes TRIO: Both

Students learn the foundational structure of a good prompt using the PREP framework (Prompt, Role, Explicit instructions, Parameters).

Learning Objectives

  • Apply the PREP method to generate specific outputs.
  • Analyze the difference between weak and strong prompts.
  • Critique AI responses for relevance.

Materials

  • Student Worksheets (PREP Template)$0.10 per student

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in literature/composition, Academic tutoring/instruction
Citations
HEA Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in Core Curriculum (Literature), 34 CFR 645.11(b)(1)(iii) - Composition and Literature, 2 CFR 200.453 - Materials and supplies costs

Teacher Guide

1.

Define PREP: Prompt (the ask), Role (persona), Explicit Instructions (format/tone), Parameters (constraints).

2.

Project a lazy prompt ('Write a story') and the result. Project a PREP prompt ('Act as a noir detective...') and compare.

3.

Challenge students to make the AI write a poem about Tacos using a specific historic style (e.g., Victorian).

3. Hallucinations: When AI Lies

Duration: 40 minutes TRIO: Both

Critical thinking session identifying when AI makes up facts ('hallucinations') and how to verify information.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify AI hallucinations.
  • Practice fact-checking methods.
  • Understand the limitations of probabilistic language models.

Materials

  • Access to Internet for Fact Checking$0

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Academic counseling/Study skills, Instruction in critical thinking
Citations
HEA Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in Study Skills, 34 CFR 645.11(a)(1) - Study skills development, 2 CFR 200.430 - Compensation - personal services

Teacher Guide

1.

Show a pre-generated AI response that cites a non-existent court case or book.

2.

Explain that LLMs do not 'know' facts; they predict the next likely word, which can lead to plausible-sounding lies.

3.

Demonstrate the 'Lateral Reading' method: checking multiple tabs to verify a single claim.

4. Few-Shot Prompting: Teaching the AI

Duration: 40 minutes TRIO: UBMS

Learning advanced prompting by providing examples (shots) within the prompt to guide the AI's pattern recognition.

Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate between Zero-shot and Few-shot prompting.
  • Create prompts that include data examples.
  • Train the AI to follow a specific format.

Materials

  • Computer Access$0

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in Lab Science, Instruction in Mathematics
Citations
HEA Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in Mathematics, 34 CFR 645.11(b)(1)(i) - Mathematics Instruction, 2 CFR 200.453 - Materials and supplies costs

Teacher Guide

Explain that providing examples (shots) helps the AI 'lock in' to a pattern without needing code.

2.

Demonstrate converting a list of messy phone numbers into a standardized format by giving the AI two correct examples first.

Guide students to apply this to language translation tasks.

5. Chain of Thought Prompting

Duration: 40 minutes TRIO: UBMS

Forcing the AI to 'show its work' to improve logic in math and science problems.

Learning Objectives

  • Use 'Let's think step by step' techniques.
  • Solve complex word problems using AI assistance.
  • Debug AI logic errors.

Materials

  • Logic Puzzle Worksheets$0

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in Mathematics, Tutoring
Citations
HEA Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in Mathematics, 34 CFR 645.11(b)(1)(i) - Mathematics Instruction, 2 CFR 200.453 - Materials and supplies costs

Teacher Guide

1.

Show a math riddle where AI often fails if asked for just the answer (e.g., the 'ball and bat cost $1.10' riddle).

2.

Explain that LLMs struggle with immediate calculation but excel when forced to reason sequentially.

3.

Instruct students to append 'Let's think step by step' to their prompts.

Texas AI Lab: Future Ready Prompting & Logic - Curriculum Narrative

Executive Summary

The 'Texas AI Lab: Future Ready Prompting & Logic' is a rigorous 20-module immersive curriculum designed to pivot high school students from passive consumers of technology to sophisticated creators and ethical evaluators of Artificial Intelligence. Spanning 13.5 instructional hours, the course integrates Large Language Model (LLM) mechanics, advanced prompt engineering, and Python-based computer science fundamentals. This curriculum is specifically engineered to bolster college readiness by simulating post-secondary STEM environments, fostering critical thinking through 'human-in-the-loop' verification, and preparing participants for high-growth career pathways in data science and AI ethics. By synthesizing Texas TEKS standards with project-based learning, the program ensures students develop the logical rigor necessary for success in competitive higher education landscapes.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is meticulously aligned with the U.S. Department of Education TRIO Upward Bound (UB) and Upward Bound Math-Science (UBMS) allowable costs and services. Under the Higher Education Act (HEA) Section 402C, the program fulfills the requirement for 'instruction in core curriculum areas' by providing advanced training in Laboratory Science (Computer Science) and Mathematics (Logic and Probability). Per 34 CFR 645.11(b)(1)(ii) and (b)(1)(i), the curriculum offers the mandatory rigorous academic preparation required for UBMS participants. Furthermore, all associated costs for instructional materials, software subscriptions, and hardware are justifiable under EDGAR/2 CFR 200.453 (Materials and Supplies) as essential for the delivery of specific educational objectives. The curriculum also supports mandatory 'Career Awareness' services as outlined in 34 CFR 645.11(a)(3) through its focused modules on AI-driven career pathways and professional development.

Standards Alignment

The Texas AI Lab curriculum is fully mapped to the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for Computer Science I & II, Digital Forensics, and Mathematics (Algebra II). Key alignments include TEKS 126.41.c.1.A regarding the creative features of emerging technologies and 111.40.c.1.B regarding mathematical problem-solving models. The program also adheres to the Texas STEM Framework, specifically prioritizing STEM Best Practices 1.1 (Engineering Design Process), 3.2 (STEM Communication), and 4.3 (Computational Thinking), ensuring that students are meeting state-level benchmarks for college and career readiness in technical fields.

Field Expeditions

To provide real-world context to the digital logic taught in the classroom, the curriculum includes three strategic field trip options within 60 miles of Dallas, Tarrant, and Johnson counties. The Perot Museum of Nature and Science (Dallas) offers hands-on engineering challenges in the TI Innovation Hall, reinforcing digital-to-physical logic. The University of Texas at Dallas (Richardson) provides essential post-secondary exposure through tours of their Computer Science MakerSpace and Machine Learning labs, meeting UBMS requirements for campus immersion. Finally, the Fort Worth Museum of Science and History‚Äôs Energy Blast exhibit provides a data-rich environment to explore the environmental impacts of technology, directly supporting the curriculum's modules on green computing and AI sustainability.

Module Narratives

The Ghost in the Machine: What is Generative AI?

This introductory module demystifies Large Language Models (LLMs) by distinguishing probabilistic text generation from traditional search engine indexing. Students establish a foundation in digital citizenship while exploring the mechanics of how machines 'learn' language, setting a professional tone for AI interaction.

Prompt Engineering 101: The PREP Method

Students are introduced to the PREP framework (Prompt, Role, Explicit instructions, Parameters), a logical structure for optimizing AI output. This activity emphasizes composition and linguistic precision, requiring students to iterate on their instructions to achieve highly specific, high-quality results.

Hallucinations: When AI Lies

This critical thinking module addresses the phenomenon of AI 'hallucinations.' Students engage in lateral reading and fact-checking exercises to identify misinformation, fostering a healthy skepticism and the research skills necessary for academic integrity in a tech-saturated world.

Few-Shot Prompting: Teaching the AI

Moving beyond simple queries, students learn 'Few-Shot' prompting by providing contextual examples to the AI. This module mirrors basic machine learning training concepts, teaching students how to use pattern recognition to automate complex data formatting tasks.

Chain of Thought Prompting

In this module, students learn to force the AI to 'show its work' through sequential reasoning. By applying this to complex math and logic puzzles, students analyze the AI‚Äôs step-by-step processing, which reinforces their own understanding of algorithmic logic and proof-based mathematics.

AI as a Debate Partner (Socratic Method)

Students use AI to simulate a Socratic dialogue, challenging their own biases on controversial STEM topics. This exercise strengthens persuasive writing and argumentation skills, as students must rebut AI-generated counter-arguments with evidence-based reasoning.

Visualizing STEM: Text-to-Image

This module bridges the gap between descriptive language and technical visualization. Students use image generators to create architectural blueprints and engineering prototypes, learning how specific technical vocabulary affects visual outputs.

AI for Data Analysis (Spreadsheets)

Focusing on data science, this module teaches students to use AI for generating complex spreadsheet formulas and interpreting raw datasets. This provides a direct application of statistics and business information management skills.

Python Pal: Intro to Coding with AI

Students transition to computational creation by using AI as a 'pair programmer.' They generate and execute Python scripts in a browser-based IDE, learning to treat the AI as a junior developer whose work must be reviewed and understood by the human 'senior dev.'

Bug Hunter: Debugging with AI

Focusing on resilience and problem-solving, this activity tasks students with fixing broken code. They learn to interpret error logs and use AI to identify syntax and logical errors, simulating real-world software development workflows.

Summarizing Scientific Papers

To enhance college-level literacy, students use AI to parse dense academic abstracts. This module teaches them how to extract methodologies and conclusions from complex texts, making high-level scientific research more accessible.

The Resume Builder

This career-readiness session shows students how to use AI to optimize their resumes for Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS). They learn to translate extracurricular activities into professional skill sets using strong action verbs.

AI for Language Learning

Students leverage AI as a conversational partner in a foreign language. By requesting grammatical corrections and native phrasing in real-time, students experience the potential of AI as a personalized tutor for linguistic mastery.

Algorithmic Bias Detective

This ethics-focused module investigates how training data can lead to biased AI outcomes. Students run experiments on gender and racial representation in AI models, discussing the social implications of technological development.

The Deepfake Challenge

Exploring the world of cybersecurity, students learn the mechanics of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). They practice identifying visual and auditory artifacts in AI-generated media to protect themselves against digital deception.

Gamifying Review: AI Quiz Maker

Students learn to use AI to improve their study habits by transforming their class notes into interactive quizzes. This module teaches them data formatting and the importance of verifying AI-generated answers against original sources.

AI for Environmental Solutions

This module explores the intersection of technology and ecology. Students research the energy costs of data centers and use AI to brainstorm sustainable solutions for local environmental issues, balancing innovation with conservation.

Capstone Part 1: Problem Definition

Students begin their final project by identifying a real-world problem. Using the engineering design process, they refine their project goals and determine how AI can serve as a primary tool in their proposed solution.

Capstone Part 2: Prototyping

Teams move into the build phase, creating functional AI prototypes‚Äîwhether through custom prompt libraries, code, or technical visualizations. This phase emphasizes iteration, collaboration, and technical documentation.

Capstone Part 3: The AI Showcase

The curriculum concludes with a formal presentation. Students demonstrate their AI solutions to a public audience, defending their technical choices and reflecting on the ethical considerations of their work, simulating a professional design review.
