The Digital Mind: The Neuroscience, Algorithms, and Analytics of Social Media (Expanded Version)

This interdisciplinary STEM curriculum bridges the gap between adolescent social media usage and academic STEM principles. Students explore the neuroscience behind addiction (Biology), the logic behind content feeds (Computer Science/Math), and the statistical impact of screen time on mental health (Data Analysis). The curriculum aligns with Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) and federal TRIO objectives.

Target: High School (Ages 14-18) • Total Time: 9 Hours (7 Classroom Modules + 1 Field Experience) • Est. Cost: $35.00

Module 1: The Dopamine Loop (Part 1) - Neuroscience & Simulation

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: Both

Students explore the biology of the VTA (Ventral Tegmental Area) and conduct a dice-rolling simulation to mimic the 'Variable Ratio Schedule' used in app design.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the role of neurotransmitters (dopamine) in the brain's reward system.
  • Simulate 'Intermittent Variable Rewards' using probability tools.

Materials

  • Dice (6-sided)$10.00 (pack of 50)
  • Poker Chips or Tokens$15.00
  • Printed 'Notification' Simulation Cards$0.00 (Self-print)

Teacher Guide

1.

Introduction (10 mins): Explain the biological purpose of dopamine (survival/seeking) vs. the modern application (scrolling). Define 'Variable Ratio Schedule' (slot machine logic).

2.

Setup (5 mins): Assign partners. Distribute dice and tokens. Explain that the 'Algorithm' student controls the rewards.

3.

Simulation Round 1 (10 mins): The 'User' attempts to refresh their feed every 5 seconds. The 'Algorithm' rolls. Only a 6 yields a token. Students record the exact time of each reward.

4.

Simulation Round 2 (10 mins): Increase the odds. Now, rolling a 4, 5, or 6 yields a token. Students record data. Save all data sheets for the next module.

Module 2: The Dopamine Loop (Part 2) - Graphing Addiction

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: Both

Using data generated in Module 1, students graph the stimulus/response frequency to visualize how unpredictability drives behavior.

Learning Objectives

  • Graph data related to stimulus and response frequencies.
  • Analyze the difference between fixed and variable ratio reinforcement.

Materials

Teacher Guide

1.

Review (5 mins): Recap the two simulation rounds (Hard to win vs. Easy to win).

2.

Graphing Instruction (10 mins): Demonstrate how to set up the axes. X-axis = Time (Scroll attempts). Y-axis = Cumulative Tokens (Total rewards).

3.

Execution (15 mins): Students plot both Round 1 and Round 2 data on the same graph using different colors.

4.

Analysis (10 mins): Facilitate a discussion. Show how Round 1 (Variable Ratio) often leads to 'burst' behavior or frustration, whereas Round 2 is steady. Connect this to the 'pull-to-refresh' mechanic in apps.

Module 3: Unboxing the Black Box (Part 1) - Algorithmic Logic Design

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: UBMS

Students begin the algorithm unit by learning logical operators and designing the 'weighting' system for a social media feed.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the logic of sorting algorithms (If/Then/Else).
  • Assign weighted variables to qualitative data.

Materials

  • Whiteboard/Projector$0.00
  • Sample 'User Profiles' (Paper)$0.00

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in Mathematics (Algebra/Logic), Exposure to Careers in Computer Science
Citations
HEA Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in mathematics and science., 34 CFR 645.11(a)(1) - Mandatory Core Curriculum., 2 CFR 200.400 - Principles for cost determination.

Teacher Guide

1.

Concept Intro (15 mins): Explain that computers don't have 'opinions'; they have math. Introduce the concept of a 'Weighted Variable'. Example: If (Post == Video) Then (Score = Score + 5).

2.

Group Setup (5 mins): Divide students into Engineering Teams. Give each team a target User Profile (e.g., 'The Gamer', 'The Activist').

3.

Formula Design (20 mins): The teams must write a linear equation to rank content. Example Formula: Total Score = (Topic Match * 5) + (Is_Video * 3) + (Is_Viral * 2). They must justify why they chose those weights.

Module 4: Unboxing the Black Box (Part 2) - Calculation & Sorting

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: Both

Students act as the 'CPU', applying their designed formulas to a set of content cards to generate a ranked feed.

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate weighted scores for data sorting.
  • Execute an algorithm manually to understand processing logic.

Materials

  • Index Cards (Content Cards with various topics)$5.00
  • Calculators$0.00 (Student phones/school inventory)

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in Mathematics (Algebra/Arithmetic)
Citations
HEA Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in mathematics., 34 CFR 645.11(a)(1) - Mandatory Core Curriculum., 2 CFR 200.453 - Materials and supplies.

Teacher Guide

1.

Setup (5 mins): Students retrieve their formulas from Module 3. Hand out the 'Content Decks' (mixed posts: news, memes, ads, friends).

2.

Processing (25 mins): This is the 'CPU' phase. Students must calculate the precise score for every card based on their User Profile. Example: Card is 'Sports Video'. Formula: (Sports=10) + (Video=5) = 15. Write '15' on the card.

3.

Sorting (10 mins): Instruct students to stack the cards physically. Highest number on top. This is the 'Feed' the user sees.

Module 5: Unboxing the Black Box (Part 3) - Bias & Ethics Analysis

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: Both

Students analyze the sorted feeds from Module 4 to identify 'Echo Chambers' and 'Filter Bubbles,' discussing the societal impact of algorithmic sorting.

Learning Objectives

  • Analyze how algorithmic personalization creates confirmation bias.
  • Evaluate the ethical implications of hidden information.

Materials

  • Sorted Card Decks (From Module 4)$0.00
  • Reflection Worksheets$0.00

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services

Citations
HEA Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in literature and society (Media Literacy)., 34 CFR 645.11(a) - Core curriculum component., 2 CFR 200.400 - Cost principles.

Teacher Guide

1.

Analysis (10 mins): Ask groups to present their 'Feed'. If the User liked Sports, is their entire feed Sports? What happened to the 'World News' card?

2.

Concept (10 mins): Explain that algorithms maximize engagement, not truth. Define 'Confirmation Bias': seeing only what you already agree with.

3.

Synthesis (20 mins): Have students answer: 'If my user only sees these top 3 cards for a year, what will they believe about the world?' Discuss the ethical responsibility of engineers to code for diversity vs. profit.

Module 6: Digital Wellness (Part 1) - The Geometry of Face Filters

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: UBMS

Students learn the engineering and geometry behind AR (Augmented Reality) face filters, focusing on mesh mapping and coordinate systems.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand how facial recognition mesh mapping works (computer vision).
  • Identify the coordinate geometry (X, Y, Z axes) used in AR.

Materials

  • Projector (for Face Mesh diagrams)$0.00
  • Optional: Smartphone with Snapchat/TikTok for demo$0.00

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in Science/Engineering (Computer Vision), Exposure to Careers in Tech
Citations
HEA Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in mathematics (Geometry)., 34 CFR 645.11(a)(1) - Mandatory Core Curriculum., 2 CFR 200.400 - Cost principles.

Teacher Guide

1.

Tech Theory (20 mins): Explain that computers see faces as a collection of points (vectors). Show a diagram of a Face Mesh. Explain that 'Beauty Filters' work by altering the Euclidean distance between points (e.g., shrinking jaw width, enlarging eye radius).

2.

Analysis (20 mins): Discuss the 'Golden Ratio' mathematically. Ask students to identify which coordinates are changed most often in filters (Cheekbones: X-axis expansion; Chin: Y-axis reduction).

Module 7: Digital Wellness (Part 2) - Statistical Analysis

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: Both

Students conduct a statistical analysis of screen time versus mood, using Excel/Google Sheets to calculate correlation coefficients.

Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate between correlation and causation.
  • Analyze scatter plots related to mental health and technology usage.

Teacher Guide

1.

Data Entry (10 mins): Provide students with a 2-column dataset (X=Hours Scrolled, Y=Anxiety 1-10). Have them type this into the spreadsheet.

2.

Visualization (15 mins): Walk through the menu: Insert > Chart > Scatter Plot. Add Trendline.

3.

Calculation (10 mins): Teach the formula '=CORREL(array1, array2)'. Explain the result (Closer to 1 = Strong Link).

4.

Conclusion (5 mins): Discuss if the phone causes anxiety, or if anxious people use phones more.

Module Narratives

Module 1: The Dopamine Loop (Part 1) - Neuroscience & Simulation

Students transition from passive users to scientific observers by simulating the biological 'Variable Ratio Schedule' of reinforcement. Through a controlled dice-rolling simulation, students replicate the neurochemical triggers of the Ventral Tegmental Area (VTA), gaining hands-on experience with probability and behavioral conditioning while exploring the rigor of laboratory science and neurobiology.

Module 2: The Dopamine Loop (Part 2) - Graphing Addiction

In this data-driven module, students convert raw behavioral observations from their simulations into cumulative record graphs. This activity emphasizes mathematical modeling and the analysis of slope as a rate of reinforcement, requiring students to interpret how unpredictability in data can influence human behavioral patterns, a skill vital for careers in statistics and UX research.

Module 3: Unboxing the Black Box (Part 1) - Algorithmic Logic Design

Students assume the role of software engineers, moving into the 'logic' phase of the curriculum. They learn to design weighted linear equations that dictate content visibility. By assigning numerical values to qualitative actions (e.g., likes vs. shares), students engage in high-level algebraic thinking and algorithmic design, establishing a foundation for computer science proficiency.

Module 4: Unboxing the Black Box (Part 2) - Calculation & Sorting

Acting as a human Central Processing Unit (CPU), students manually execute their custom algorithms. This module demands mathematical precision and adherence to the order of operations as students calculate 'relevance scores' for content. This rigorous exercise demystifies the 'black box' of technology, demonstrating that digital experiences are the result of deterministic mathematical processes.

Module 5: Unboxing the Black Box (Part 3) - Bias & Ethics Analysis

This module challenges students to evaluate the societal and ethical consequences of their engineering choices. By analyzing the 'echo chambers' created by their sorted feeds, students engage in critical discourse regarding confirmation bias and digital equity. This unit connects STEM technical skills with the social science rigor required for tech policy and AI ethics careers.

Module 6: Digital Wellness (Part 1) - The Geometry of Face Filters

Students explore the intersection of geometry and computer vision by deconstructing Augmented Reality (AR) face meshes. By mapping 3D coordinate points onto 2D surfaces, students apply geometric transformations to understand how computer vision algorithms modify human features, providing a sophisticated look at 3D modeling and digital engineering.

Module 7: Digital Wellness (Part 2) - Statistical Analysis

The curriculum concludes with a professional-grade statistical lab where students utilize spreadsheet software to calculate correlation coefficients (r-values) between screen time and mental health indicators. Students learn the critical distinction between correlation and causation, equipping them with the data literacy skills necessary for college-level research and public health analysis.
