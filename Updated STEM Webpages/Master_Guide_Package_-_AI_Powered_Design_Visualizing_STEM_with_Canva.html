STEM Architect Master Guide

AI-Powered Design: Visualizing STEM with Canva

This comprehensive 10-module curriculum leverages Canva's Artificial Intelligence tools (Magic Media, Magic Write, Magic Design, and Data Visualization) to teach High School students how to visualize complex scientific concepts, rapid-prototype engineering solutions, and communicate technical data. Designed specifically for TRIO Upward Bound and UBMS programs, the curriculum bridges the gap between digital literacy and rigorous STEM instruction, moving students from the ethics of AI to practical application in Biology, Chemistry, Physics, Engineering, and Mathematics.

Target: High School (Ages 14-18) • Total Time: 10 Hours (Ten 60-minute sessions) • Est. Cost: $7.00

1

Visualizing the Invisible: AI in Scientific Modeling

Duration: 60 Minutes TRIO: Both

Students use Canva's 'Magic Media' (Text-to-Image AI) to generate visualizations of microscopic biological structures or astrophysical phenomena. They will compare AI-generated images against textbook scientific diagrams to evaluate accuracy, limitations, and the role of AI in scientific communication.

Learning Objectives

  • Define Generative AI and explain its potential applications in STEM fields.
  • Utilize prompt engineering to generate scientific imagery using Canva Magic Media.
  • Critique AI-generated images for scientific accuracy compared to peer-reviewed sources.
  • Design a comparative infographic showcasing the capabilities and limitations of AI modeling.

Materials

  • Laptops/Chromebooks with Internet Access$0 (Existing Equipment/Facility Cost)
  • Canva for Education Accounts$0 (Free for K-12 Schools)
  • Projector/Smartboard$0 (Existing Equipment)

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in Laboratory Science (Bio/Chem/Physics concepts), Exposure to the range of career options (Scientific Illustration/Data Science), Instruction in literature or composition (Technical Writing/Prompting)

Teacher Guide

1.

Begin with a 5-minute discussion on how scientists visualize things we cannot see (e.g., atoms, black holes).

2.

Demonstrate Canva's 'Magic Media' tool. Show how changing the text prompt alters the image output.

3.

Assign students a scientific subject (e.g., 'Mitochondria structure', 'Electron cloud model', 'Surface of Mars').

4.

Instruct students to generate three iterations of their subject using different prompts.

5.

Guide students to drag their best AI image into a design canvas and upload a textbook diagram of the same subject next to it.

6.

Facilitate a closing discussion on the differences. Ask: 'Where did the AI fail? Where did it succeed?'

Curriculum Narrative: AI-Powered Design: Visualizing STEM with Canva

Executive Summary

The 'AI-Powered Design: Visualizing STEM with Canva' curriculum is a rigorous 10-module instructional program designed specifically for High School students (ages 14-18) within TRIO Upward Bound and Upward Bound Math-Science (UBMS) projects. This program leverages cutting-edge generative artificial intelligence tools‚Äîincluding Magic Media, Magic Write, and Magic Design‚Äîto bridge the gap between digital literacy and core STEM competencies. Over 10 contact hours, students move from understanding the ethics of AI to practical application in biology, chemistry, physics, and engineering. By synthesizing complex scientific data into professional visualizations, students develop the technical communication and critical thinking skills essential for postsecondary success in competitive STEM majors.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is meticulously aligned with the Higher Education Act (HEA) Section 402C(b) and 402C(e), which mandates instruction in mathematics, laboratory science, and computer science for TRIO Upward Bound programs. It adheres to 34 CFR Part 645.11 by providing direct instruction in laboratory science (Biology/Chemistry/Physics concepts) and tech-based electives that prepare students for careers in which persons from disadvantaged backgrounds are underrepresented. All associated costs, including computing devices and software access, are governed by the cost principles of 2 CFR 200 (EDGAR), specifically 2 CFR 200.453 for materials and supplies and 2 CFR 200.461 for publication and printing of student-generated research. The program fulfills the UBMS-specific requirement for a 'rigorous' curriculum that includes exposure to university-level research environments and data analysis.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is aligned with the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for High School Technology Applications (¬ß126.41), Science (¬ß112.34, ¬ß112.35), and CTE-STEM (¬ß130.362). Specific benchmarks addressed include the creation and editing of digital graphics, the evaluation of scientific research impact, and the use of engineering graphics to communicate technical information. Furthermore, the modules integrate the Texas STEM Framework, specifically focusing on the strands of Design Thinking, Digital Literacy, and Scientific Visualization to ensure students meet state-level college and career readiness standards.

Field Expeditions

The curriculum includes strategic field experiences within the Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex to reinforce classroom learning. Students may visit the Perot Museum of Nature and Science in Dallas County to explore the 'Being Human' hall, connecting professional museum visualization techniques to their work in medical imaging and scientific modeling. Additionally, a visit to the University of Texas at Arlington (UTA) FabLab in Tarrant County provides exposure to a top-tier university fabrication laboratory. These trips offer high-value educational engagement by allowing students to see the real-world application of digital design in engineering and biological research, meeting the TRIO requirement for exposure to university campuses and STEM career environments.

Module Narratives

Visualizing the Invisible: AI in Scientific Modeling

Students engage in prompt engineering to generate visualizations of microscopic structures and astrophysical phenomena using Magic Media. The activity emphasizes academic rigor by requiring students to critique AI-generated images against textbook diagrams, fostering a deep understanding of scientific accuracy and the limitations of generative modeling.

Rapid Prototyping: Engineering Solutions Pitch Deck

Acting as engineering consultants, students utilize 'Magic Write' to brainstorm solutions to community infrastructure challenges. They transform these concepts into professional pitch decks, learning to reconcile AI-generated suggestions with realistic engineering constraints, budgets, and material requirements.

Data Storytelling: AI-Enhanced Statistical Analysis

Focusing on the 'M' in STEM, this module teaches students how to import raw datasets into Canva to create statistically accurate charts. Students use AI to summarize trends and extract key insights, developing the visual literacy skills necessary to communicate complex data without misleading the audience.

Elemental Ethics: Designing Chemical Safety Protocols

In this chemistry-focused module, students research hazardous substances and design safety posters. While AI generates the thematic backgrounds, students must manually verify and place Globally Harmonized System (GHS) symbols, reinforcing the importance of human oversight and accuracy in safety engineering.

Evolutionary Forecast: AI Modeling of Future Ecosystems

Students apply principles of natural selection and climate science to predict species adaptation over time. By using AI to visualize 'evolved' animals based on specific environmental constraints, students demonstrate a mastery of biological traits and ecological forecasting.

Mission to Mars: Branding a Space Expedition

This module combines physics and aerospace engineering concepts with graphic design. Students create mission patches for a Mars colony, ensuring their designs reflect planetary physics (such as gravity and atmospheric density) while learning the importance of public communication in scientific funding.

Algorithmic Logic: AI Flowcharting for Coding

Students develop computational thinking skills by decomposing everyday tasks into logic-based flowcharts. Using Canva‚Äôs Whiteboard, they map out decision trees and use AI to identify 'edge cases,' providing a foundation for computer science and boolean logic.

Medical Imaging: Augmented Reality Posters

Students simulate the career of a medical illustrator by creating anatomical posters. They prompt AI for detailed 3D organ renders and organize them into system-level views, requiring high-level anatomical knowledge to ensure the AI-generated imagery is biologically correct.

Green Cities: Urban Planning with AI Generative Fill
