STEM Architect Master Guide

Alley Cats STEM Academy: Applied Physics & Geometry (Refined Micro-Modules)

A rigorous, experiential learning curriculum situated in a 'living laboratory' environment (Bowling, Mini-Golf, Arcade). This curriculum is specifically designed for DOE TRIO programs to ensure strict separation between entertainment and education. It utilizes 21 distinct micro-activities to satisfy HEA requirements for instruction in mathematics and science. Students engage in data acquisition, statistical analysis, and geometric modeling using high-interest mechanics.

Target: High School (Ages 14-18) • Total Time: 12.5 Hours (Total Instructional Time) • Est. Cost: $17.85

Bowling Module 1: The Need for Speed (Kinematics)

Duration: 45 Minutes TRIO: Both

Students utilize the bowling lane as a friction-variable track to calculate average velocity, differentiating between the oil pattern (slide) and the dry backend (roll).

Learning Objectives

  • Measure time accurately using digital stopwatches.
  • Calculate average velocity using v = d/t.
  • Convert units from feet/second to meters/second.

Materials

  • Digital Stopwatch$0 (Student Phone)
  • Lane Rental (Lab Fee)$2.00 per student
  • Data Collection Log$0.10

Allowable Services

Teacher Guide

1.

2.

Instruct students to convert 60 feet to meters (approx 18.28m) before calculation.

3.

Ensure students differentiate between the time the ball is released and when it crosses the foul line.

Bowling Module 2: Heavy Hitters (Momentum & Energy)

Duration: 45 Minutes TRIO: Both

Students analyze the relationship between mass and momentum by using bowling balls of varying weights (8lb vs 14lb) to observe impact force and pin scatter.

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate momentum (p=mv).
  • Compare Kinetic Energy (KE=0.5mv^2) vs Momentum.
  • Observe elastic vs inelastic collisions.

Materials

  • House Balls (8lb, 14lb)$0 (Included in rental)
  • Calculator$0

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in Science

Teacher Guide

1.

Instruct students to find a 'Light' ball (6-8lbs) and a 'Heavy' ball (12-14lbs).

2.

3.

Ask students to attempt to throw both balls at the same speed.

Alley Cats STEM Academy: Applied Physics & Geometry ‚Äì A Curriculum Narrative for DOE TRIO Programs

Executive Summary

The Alley Cats STEM Academy is a rigorous, experiential learning curriculum designed specifically for federally funded TRIO Upward Bound and Upward Bound Math-Science programs. By utilizing 'living laboratory' environments‚Äîincluding bowling lanes, miniature golf courses, and interactive arcade systems‚Äîthis program transforms recreational spaces into high-level physics and geometry laboratories. The curriculum consists of 21 refined micro-modules that facilitate hands-on data acquisition, statistical modeling, and mechanical analysis, ensuring that participants develop the quantitative skills necessary for success in postsecondary STEM degrees.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is designed for strict alignment with the Higher Education Act (HEA) of 1965, Section 402C(b), which mandates that TRIO programs provide instruction in mathematics and laboratory science. Per 34 CFR 645.11(a)(1), these modules fulfill the requirement for a core academic curriculum. Financial expenditures related to facility rentals (lane fees, course admission, and arcade-based data credits) are justified as 'Instructional Lab Fees' necessary for the delivery of the academic component, meeting the 'Necessary and Reasonable' cost principles outlined in EDGAR (2 CFR 200.403) and the 'Allocability' standards of 2 CFR 200.405, as the costs are incurred specifically for the educational activities described herein.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is meticulously mapped to the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for Physics (including 2E, 4B, 4C, 4D, 5B, 6C, 6D, 7A, 7B, 7D), Geometry (2A, 2B, 4B, 5A), and Statistics (2A, 2D, 5A, 5B). By integrating these state standards into real-world applications, the academy supports the Texas STEM Framework's goals of fostering STEM fluency, critical thinking, and computational modeling. Each activity is designed to prepare students for the academic rigor of high-stakes testing while providing practical exposure to career-aligned skills.

Field Expeditions

Field laboratory sessions are conducted at Alley Cats Entertainment facilities located in Arlington and Hurst, Texas. These venues are strategically situated within 60 miles of Dallas, Tarrant, and Johnson counties, minimizing transport overhead for local TRIO programs. The facility provides unique physical apparatus‚Äîsuch as 60-foot friction-variable lanes, complex topographic putting greens, and ballistic arcade simulators‚Äîthat serve as essential instruments for experiments in kinematics, optics, and structural geometry that cannot be replicated in a standard classroom setting.

Module Narratives

Bowling Module 1: The Need for Speed (Kinematics)

Students utilize professional bowling lanes as friction-variable tracks to master the principles of linear kinematics. By measuring time intervals over a fixed 18.28-meter distance, students calculate average velocity and learn to differentiate between sliding and rolling motion, bridging the gap between abstract formulas (v=d/t) and physical data acquisition.

Bowling Module 2: Heavy Hitters (Momentum & Energy)

This module explores the relationship between mass, velocity, and impact force. Students use varying bowling ball weights to observe and calculate momentum (p=mv) and kinetic energy, analyzing how mass affects pin scatter and energy transfer in elastic and inelastic collisions.

Bowling Module 3: Coefficient of Friction

Focusing on tribology, students calculate the coefficient of kinetic friction between the ball and the lane surface. By observing deceleration across the oiled front and dry backend of the lane, students solve for unknowns in force diagrams, mirroring high-level engineering challenges in materials science.

Bowling Module 4: Rotational Energy (Spin)

Students utilize slow-motion video analysis to estimate angular velocity (RPM). This advanced module introduces the moment of inertia for a solid sphere, challenging students to calculate rotational kinetic energy and understand the complex interplay between translational and rotational motion.

Bowling Module 5: Acoustic Physics (Speed of Sound)

By measuring the temporal delay between the visual impact of a ball and the auditory arrival of the sound wave, students calculate the speed of sound. This activity emphasizes experimental error analysis and the properties of wave propagation in a multi-sensory environment.

Bowling Module 6: Probability of the Spare

This module introduces empirical probability through the lens of sports data. Students record remaining pin configurations (leaves) to create frequency tables and calculate the statistical likelihood of specific spares, aligning with data science and actuarial career pathways.

Bowling Module 7: Biomechanics of the Swing

Students model the human arm as a simple pendulum to analyze period and frequency. By comparing theoretical periods based on arm length to actual swing times, participants explore the biophysics of muscle torque and harmonic motion.

Golf Module 1: The Slope of the Green

Students apply civil engineering surveying techniques to calculate the 'rise over run' of putting surfaces. This module reinforces the geometric distance and slope formulas by requiring students to measure vertical change and horizontal distance in a three-dimensional field.

Golf Module 2: The Law of Reflection (Bank Shots)

By applying the Law of Reflection, students predict the path of a golf ball banking off a wall. The module uses the 'phantom hole' concept to teach geometric transformations, symmetry, and the relationship between angles of incidence and reflection.

Golf Module 3: Centripetal Force

This advanced activity analyzes curved paths and loops to identify the centripetal force required to maintain a trajectory. Students explore the relationship between the radius of curvature and velocity, simulating the logic used in highway and structural design.

Golf Module 4: Impulse & Momentum

Students investigate how the duration of contact between a putter and the ball influences velocity. By exploring the formula J=Ft, students learn the physics of impulse and why follow-through is a critical variable in both sports and mechanical engineering.

Golf Module 5: Periodic Motion (Timing Obstacles)

Using moving obstacles such as windmills, students calculate period (T) and frequency (f) to determine optimal timing windows. This reinforces the relationship T=1/f and introduces the concept of phase and periodic functions in physical systems.

Golf Module 6: Vector Addition

Complex putts across sloped greens are broken down into x and y vector components. Students must mathematically determine a resultant vector that accounts for both the initial aim and the gravitational acceleration caused by the slope, mirroring flight dynamics.

Golf Module 7: Friction & Deceleration

Students conduct a comparative analysis of friction by measuring the rolling distance of balls on turf versus rough surfaces. This investigation into materials science helps students visualize energy loss to heat and the impact of texture on kinetic energy.

Arcade Module 1: Expected Value (Skeeball Statistics)

Utilizing Skeeball data, students calculate the Expected Value (EV) of various scoring strategies. This advanced statistical module forces students to evaluate risk vs. reward through a discrete random variable distribution, a core competency in finance and data analysis.

Arcade Module 2: Basketball Projectile Motion

Students use smartphone-based video analysis to trace the parabolic flight of a basketball. By identifying the vertex and release point, students derive the quadratic equation for their shot, directly linking Algebra II concepts to physical ballistics.

Arcade Module 3: 2D Momentum (Air Hockey)

Air hockey puck collisions provide a laboratory for observing conservation of momentum in two dimensions. Students use video capture to sketch vector components before and after impact, verifying the elastic properties of collisions in a near-frictionless system.

Arcade Module 4: Human Reaction Time

By interacting with reflex-based gaming systems, students collect data on neural reaction times. This activity integrates biology and statistics, as students analyze the speed of signal transmission across the human nervous system compared to group averages.

Arcade Module 5: Circuits & Logic (Pinball/Pacman)

Students diagram the logic flow of arcade cabinets, identifying inputs (buttons), processes (Boolean logic), and outputs (screen actions). This computer science module introduces students to hardware-software interfaces and if/then algorithmic thinking.

Arcade Module 6: Gear Ratios (Racing Sims)

Using racing simulators, students observe the trade-off between torque and speed. By graphing engine RPM against vehicle velocity across different gears, students analyze mechanical advantage and the linear relationships found in automotive engineering.

Arcade Module 7: Light & Optics (Gun Games)

This module explores the technology behind light-gun sensors. Students investigate line-of-sight, IR sensor calibration, and optical positioning, applying Euclidean geometry to understand how computers track movement in 3D space.
