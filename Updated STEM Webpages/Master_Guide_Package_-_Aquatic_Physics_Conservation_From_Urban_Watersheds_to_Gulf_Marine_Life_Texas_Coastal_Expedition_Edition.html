Aquatic Physics & Conservation: From Urban Watersheds to Gulf Marine Life (Texas Coastal Expedition Edition)

An advanced, multi-day STEM curriculum connecting North Texas urban watersheds to the Texas Gulf Coast. This expanded modular program begins with classroom physics and local water analysis in the DFW area, followed by a residential field expedition to Galveston and Corpus Christi. Students investigate bioacoustics, fluid dynamics, and estuarine ecology, directly observing the Trinity River's termination point in Galveston Bay. This curriculum meets strict DOE TRIO compliance for summer residential components or rigorous academic field studies.

Notes & Observations

Aquatic Physics & Conservation: From Urban Watersheds to Gulf Marine Life (Texas Coastal Expedition Edition)

Executive Summary

This comprehensive STEM curriculum provides a rigorous, multi-disciplinary exploration of the hydrological and ecological connection between the North Texas urban corridor and the Gulf Coast ecosystem. Designed for high-achieving high school students, the program bridges the gap between theoretical classroom physics and applied field research. Through a sequence of six modules, students progress from fundamental wave mechanics and digital bioacoustics to on-site estuarine analysis and population modeling. The curriculum culminates in a residential field expedition to the Texas coast, where students engage with higher education faculty and industry professionals, reinforcing the STEM pipeline and fostering environmental stewardship through the lens of physics and engineering.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This program is specifically designed to meet the rigorous requirements for allowable services under the Higher Education Act (HEA) of 1965, Section 402C, and the associated regulations in 34 CFR Part 645. Specifically, the curriculum satisfies 34 CFR 645.11(b) by providing required instruction in laboratory science and mathematics, and 34 CFR 645.11(c) regarding summer residential components that simulate the college experience. All associated costs, including instructional supplies, transportation for educational field trips, and student per diem, are structured to remain compliant with EDGAR cost principles as outlined in 2 CFR 200.403 (necessary and reasonable), 2 CFR 200.453 (materials and supplies), and 2 CFR 200.474 (travel costs), ensuring all expenditures directly support the project's stated educational objectives and are allocable to the grant's goals.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is meticulously aligned with the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for Physics, Biology, Aquatic Science, and Statistics, ensuring high academic rigor. Specific alignments include Physics 7(A) and 7(B) regarding wave propagation and characteristics, IPC 5(G) for energy transfer, and Aquatic Science 10(A) and 12(B) concerning water pollutants and human impact on aquatic species. Furthermore, the program integrates the Texas STEM Framework by prioritizing Scientific Inquiry, Technology Integration, and Social Responsibility, providing students with a multifaceted educational experience that exceeds standard secondary requirements and prepares them for post-secondary STEM success.

Field Expeditions

The curriculum leverages high-impact experiential learning through strategic field sites. Within the immediate DFW area (Dallas/Tarrant/Johnson counties), the Dallas World Aquarium serves as a critical baseline for comparative river-marine studies and life-support engineering. The expedition then expands to the Texas Gulf Coast, including a visit to Texas A&M University at Galveston (TAMUG) to expose students to maritime college life and estuarine ecology. Finally, the Texas State Aquarium in Corpus Christi provides a unique laboratory for studying the intersection of physics and veterinary science. These sites were selected for their ability to provide direct access to professional STEM environments and academic mentorship, justifying the travel as essential to the curriculum's rigor.

Module Narratives

Module 1: Fundamentals of Wave Physics

This module establishes a rigorous academic foundation by investigating the mechanical properties of wave propagation. Students utilize physical manipulatives to differentiate between transverse and longitudinal waves, applying conceptual modeling to understand how medium density‚Äîsuch as the transition from North Texas air to Gulf seawater‚Äîalters wave speed and energy transfer. This foundational physics unit ensures students possess the theoretical framework necessary for subsequent bioacoustics and sonar analysis.

Module 2: Digital Bioacoustics Laboratory

Transitioning from theory to technology, this module introduces students to digital signal processing and acoustical engineering. Utilizing professional-grade software like Audacity and NOAA hydrophone data, students analyze marine mammal vocalizations to calculate distance via time-delay constants. This laboratory experience emphasizes the impact of anthropogenic noise pollution on marine communication, directly connecting algebraic manipulation to real-world environmental challenges and careers in acoustical engineering.

Module 3: TAMUG Estuary Analysis (Galveston Field Lab)

In this immersive field lab, students conduct on-site chemical and physical analysis of the Trinity River's discharge into the Galveston Bay estuary. Students utilize refractometers and field probes to measure salinity gradients, pH, and dissolved oxygen, correlating water density with ecological health. This activity, conducted in proximity to the Texas A&M University at Galveston campus, provides vital exposure to collegiate-level field research and the career pathways of hydrologists and wetland ecologists.

Module 4: Corpus Christi Rehabilitation Engineering

Students investigate the practical application of physics in veterinary medicine at the Texas State Aquarium Wildlife Rescue Center. By analyzing trauma cases like sea turtle boat strikes, students apply free-body diagrams to understand buoyancy disorders and the engineering of life-support systems. This module focuses on the intersection of human-made mechanical force and biological consequences, highlighting careers in biomedical engineering and aquatic veterinary science.

Module 5: Statistical Modeling (Mark-Recapture)

This advanced mathematics module challenges students to estimate wildlife populations using the Lincoln-Petersen index. Students build digital models in Excel to process field data, calculating percent error and standard deviation to evaluate data validity. This module reinforces the 'Math' in STEM, teaching students that rigorous data analysis is the bedrock of scientific conservation and fisheries management, while developing essential spreadsheet literacy for college readiness.

Module 6: Capstone - The Trinity Connection

The capstone project requires a synthesis of the entire curriculum, where students trace the 'Trinity Connection' from DFW storm drains to the Gulf Coast. Using GIS mapping and design-thinking principles, students develop prototypes or policy solutions to mitigate urban pollution. This final presentation-based activity fosters critical thinking and social responsibility, requiring students to defend their scientific arguments and engineering designs to their peers and instructors.
