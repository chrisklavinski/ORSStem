STEM Architect Master Guide

Architectural Engineering & Design: From Foundations to Future Skylines

A comprehensive 20-lesson STEM curriculum exploring the intersection of art, engineering, and mathematics through architecture. Designed specifically for DOE TRIO programs (UB/UBMS) in Texas, this course scaffolds students from historical foundations to structural physics, transitioning into modern CAD technology and sustainable design. The curriculum emphasizes college readiness in STEM fields, utilizing Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) and fulfilling federal grant objectives regarding rigorous curriculum and exposure to postsecondary programs.

Target: High School (Ages 14-18) • Total Time: 20 Sessions (40-60 Minutes each) • Est. Cost: $218.80

1. Form Follows Function: Introduction to Architecture

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: Both

Students define architecture and analyze everyday buildings to understand the relationship between aesthetic form and practical function.

Learning Objectives

  • Define architecture and distinguish it from construction.
  • Analyze a structure based on the principle of 'Form Follows Function'.
  • Identify key architectural styles in their local community.

Materials

  • Sketchbooks (Architectural Journals)$2.00 per student
  • Graphite Pencils (HB, 2B) and Erasers$0.50 per student

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Exposure to academic programs, Mentoring
Citations
Section 402C(b) (Academic improvement services), 34 CFR 645.11(a)(1) (Curriculum improvement), 34 CFR 645.43(a) (Upward Bound Services), 2 CFR 200.453 (Supplies: Sketchbooks are necessary for the performance of the award)

Teacher Guide

1.

Present slides showing contrasting buildings (e.g., a Gothic cathedral vs. a modern office).

2.

Lead a discussion on why buildings look the way they do, introducing the phrase 'Form Follows Function' (Louis Sullivan).

3.

Introduce the sketchbooks as their 'Architectural Journal' for the course, emphasizing that architects think through drawing.

2. Ancient Engineering: The Geometry of Pyramids

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: UBMS

Exploration of the structural stability of the pyramid shape and the mathematics behind calculating volume and surface area.

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate the slope, volume, and surface area of a pyramid.
  • Understand why the pyramid shape was structurally necessary for ancient civilizations.

Materials

  • Heavy Cardstock$5.00 per class
  • Masking Tape$2.00 per class
  • Sand (for load testing)$5.00 per class

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in Mathematics, Tutoring
Citations
Section 402C(b) (Core curriculum instruction), 34 CFR 645.11(b)(1) (Instruction in Math), 34 CFR 645.11(b)(2) (Instruction in Science), 2 CFR 200.453

Teacher Guide

1.

Review the geometric formulas for pyramids (Volume = 1/3 Base x Height).

2.

Discuss the distribution of weight in a wide-base structure versus a vertical column.

3.

Challenge students to build the tallest paper pyramid that can hold a 1lb sandbag.

Curriculum Narrative: Architectural Engineering & Design: From Foundations to Future Skylines

Executive Summary

Architectural Engineering & Design: From Foundations to Future Skylines is a rigorous, 20-lesson STEM curriculum designed to bridge the gap between abstract mathematical concepts and tangible career pathways in the built environment. Targeted at high school participants in DOE TRIO Upward Bound and UBMS programs, the course scaffolds learners from the historical physics of ancient structures to modern computer-aided design (CAD) and sustainable urban planning. By integrating hands-on laboratory science with technical drafting and financial literacy, the curriculum prepares students for postsecondary success in architecture, civil engineering, and construction management while fostering a deep understanding of the intersection between art and structural physics.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is designed in strict adherence to the Higher Education Act (HEA) Section 402C(b), which mandates that Upward Bound projects provide instruction in core subject areas, including mathematics through pre-calculus and laboratory science. Under 34 CFR Part 645.11 and 645.43, the services provided‚Äîincluding exposure to STEM careers and academic tutoring‚Äîare qualifying activities. All associated costs, including consumable laboratory materials (cement, cardstock, and drafting tools) and educational software, align with the 'necessary and reasonable' cost principles outlined in EDGAR/2 CFR 200.453 regarding materials and supplies. This program specifically supports the UBMS mandate for intensive math and science instruction to encourage entry into STEM professions.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is meticulously mapped to the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for Principles of Architecture (130.42), Architectural Design I (130.46), and Engineering Design (130.362). Furthermore, it integrates Mathematics TEKS for Geometry (111.43) and Algebra I (111.39) through practical application in scale and load-bearing calculations. The instructional design follows the Texas STEM Framework, emphasizing Inquiry-Based Learning (TS.3.A), Communication of Ideas (TS.5.B), and the Engineering Design Process (TS.1.C), ensuring that students develop the cognitive complexity required for the Texas Success Initiative (TSI) and subsequent college-level coursework.

Field Expeditions

Students will engage in high-impact experiential learning through strategic field trips within the Dallas, Tarrant, and Johnson County corridor. Proposed site visits include the University of Texas at Arlington College of Architecture, Planning, and Public Affairs (CAPPA) to visualize postsecondary pathways; the Perot Museum of Nature and Science Engineering Hall to observe dynamic structural physics; and the Kimbell Art Museum in Fort Worth to analyze Louis Kahn‚Äôs revolutionary use of geometry and natural light. These excursions fulfill federal requirements for exposure to postsecondary programs while reinforcing lesson-specific concepts like solar studies and seismic engineering through direct observation of professional-grade facilities.

Module Narratives

1. Form Follows Function: Introduction to Architecture

Students begin by exploring the philosophical and practical foundations of architecture, analyzing how the intended use of a space dictates its aesthetic form. This introductory session establishes the 'Architectural Journal' as a tool for inquiry, requiring students to use observation and sketching to classify local architectural styles and redesign objects for new functions.

2. Ancient Engineering: The Geometry of Pyramids

This module transitions into the mathematics of ancient structures, where students apply algebraic formulas to calculate the volume, surface area, and slope of pyramids. The lesson culminates in a weight-distribution challenge that tests the structural integrity of paper prototypes under the stress of static loads.

3. The Golden Ratio in Classical Architecture

Focusing on the intersection of mathematics and beauty, students investigate the Parthenon and Greek orders to derive the Golden Ratio (Phi). Through the use of calipers and high-resolution architectural prints, students practice precision measurement and apply algebraic ratios to verify classical aesthetic proportions.

4. The Roman Arch: Keystone Physics

This physics-focused lab challenges students to build self-supporting voussoir arches. By analyzing force vectors and the role of the keystone, students learn how vertical gravity is converted into lateral thrust, a foundational concept for understanding modern civil engineering and masonry.

5. Gothic Engineering: Flying Buttresses

Students explore the evolution of structural supports by analyzing the limitations of Romanesque thick-walled construction. Through hands-on modeling with cardboard and weights, they design and test flying buttresses to prevent wall collapse, demonstrating how structural innovation allows for the creation of open, light-filled spaces.

6. Tension vs. Compression: Bridge Building I

This lesson introduces the two primary forces acting on all structures through truss analysis. Students use color-coded diagrams and craft-stick modeling to identify members under tension and compression, emphasizing the geometric rigidity of the triangle in modern bridge design.

7. Blueprint Basics: Scale and Measurement

Developing vocational skills, students learn to use an architect's scale ruler to interpret technical drawings. This module focuses on the mathematical conversion of real-world measurements to 1/4-inch scale, culminating in the creation of a scaled floor plan of their own classroom.

8. Orthographic Projection: Elevations

To improve spatial visualization, students utilize the 'Glass Box' theory to project 3D objects into 2D drafting space. Students document complex shapes by drawing top, front, and side elevations, a core competency required for collegiate architectural and mechanical engineering programs.

9. Material Science: Concrete Lab

In this chemistry-integrated lab, students explore the hydration process and exothermic reactions involved in concrete production. By mixing various ratios of aggregate and cement, students observe how chemical composition affects the strength and durability of structural building materials.

10. Earthquake Engineering: Shake Tables

Students apply geophysics and structural analysis to design buildings capable of withstanding lateral seismic loads. Utilizing custom shake tables, students test their prototypes for resonance and deformation, iterating on their designs through the addition of cross-bracing and tuned mass dampers.

11. Digital Design: Intro to CAD (Tinkercad)

Introducing modern industry standards, this lesson provides a foundation in Computer-Aided Design (CAD). Students learn to navigate a 3D workplane, utilizing additive and subtractive modeling techniques to create digital architectural prototypes, fostering critical digital literacy.

12. 3D Modeling: Interior Walls

Building upon CAD basics, students design complex interior layouts while considering real-world constraints such as wall thickness and circulation. This module emphasizes precision and the use of technical measurement tools within a digital environment to create functional 2-bedroom floor plans.

13. Site Analysis: Topography

This lesson integrates earth science by teaching students to interpret topographical maps. Students construct physical landscape models using layered cardboard to represent contour intervals, determining optimal building placement based on slope and environmental conditions.

14. Solar Studies: Orientation and Light

Students analyze the sun's path to optimize building orientation for energy efficiency. By simulating seasonal sun angles with light sources, they learn to design passive solar features like awnings and south-facing windows to minimize heat gain and maximize natural light.

15. HVAC & Airflow: Ventilation Simulation

Focusing on thermodynamics and fluid dynamics, this lab uses visual smoke simulations to demonstrate the 'Stack Effect' and cross-ventilation. Students learn how air pressure and temperature differentials influence the design of healthy, breathable building interiors.

16. Sustainable Design: The Passive House

Students synthesize their knowledge of insulation, orientation, and materials to design a 'Passive House' that requires minimal energy. This module introduces LEED standards and challenges students to produce a detailed cross-section of an eco-friendly structure.

17. Project Management: Cost Estimation

This financial literacy module applies arithmetic and spreadsheet logic to construction budgeting. Students calculate the surface areas of a renovation project and apply unit pricing to create a professional-grade cost estimate, including contingency funds for waste.

18. Landscape Architecture: Zoning and Flow

Broadening the scope to the outdoor environment, students explore landscape design and xeriscaping. They learn to balance hardscape and softscape elements, utilizing native Texas plant species to create functional and aesthetically pleasing site plans.

19. Urban Planning: The Neighborhood Layout

Students transition from individual buildings to community systems, exploring the principles of zoning and the '15-minute city.' Collaborating in 'City Councils,' they design walkable neighborhood maps that integrate infrastructure, housing, and civic spaces.

20. Capstone: The Dream Community Center

The curriculum concludes with a formal design jury. Students apply all technical, mathematical, and aesthetic concepts to design a community center, presenting their final plans, models, and engineering justifications to an audience to demonstrate mastery of the curriculum's objectives.
