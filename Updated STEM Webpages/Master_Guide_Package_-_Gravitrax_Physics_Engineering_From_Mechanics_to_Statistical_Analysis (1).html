STEM Architect Master Guide

Gravitrax Physics & Engineering: From Mechanics to Statistical Analysis

A 20-lesson comprehensive STEM curriculum for High School students (Upward Bound/UBMS) using Gravitrax marble run systems to explore Newtonian physics, kinetic and potential energy, engineering design constraints, and statistical quality control. The curriculum progresses from basic mechanical concepts to complex systems engineering and statistical data analysis, aligned with Texas TEKS for Physics and Mathematics.

Target: High School (Ages 14-18) • Total Time: 800 minutes (20 sessions x 40 minutes) • Est. Cost: $165.00

Activity 1: Introduction to Potential Energy & System Components

Duration: 40 minutes TRIO: Both

Students explore the Gravitrax components and establish the fundamental relationship between height (gravitational potential energy) and motion.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify and classify mechanical components of the Gravitrax system.
  • Define Gravitational Potential Energy (PEg).
  • Calculate PEg based on height measurements of the marble start point.

Materials

  • Gravitrax Pro Starter Set (1 per group of 3)$80.00
  • Metric Rulers/Tape Measure$5.00
  • Digital Scale (to weigh marbles)$15.00

2.

Challenge students to build a simple track with the highest possible starting point using only the pillars provided.

Ensure students measure height in meters, not centimeters, for the Joule calculation.

Activity 2: Kinetic Energy & Conservation Principles

Duration: 40 minutes TRIO: Both

Students convert potential energy into kinetic energy, calculating theoretical vs. actual velocity to identify energy loss.

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate Kinetic Energy (KE).
  • Apply the Law of Conservation of Energy.
  • Analyze why theoretical velocity differs from actual velocity (friction).

Materials

  • Gravitrax Kit$0.00
  • Stopwatch (or Phone)$0.00

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Instruction in Laboratory Science, Tutoring
Citations
HEA Sec. 402C(b) - Instruction in science, 34 CFR 645.11(b) - Academic tutoring, 2 CFR 200.404 - Reasonable costs for program objectives

Teacher Guide

1.

Review the formula for Kinetic Energy (KE = 0.5 * m * v^2).

2.

Have students equate Initial PE to Final KE to solve for theoretical velocity.

3.

Have students time the run over a known distance to find average velocity.

4.

Lead discussion: Why is the measured velocity lower than the calculated velocity?

Curriculum Narrative: Gravitrax Physics & Engineering: From Mechanics to Statistical Analysis

Executive Summary

This 20-session STEM curriculum is designed to provide high school students, particularly those in Upward Bound and Upward Bound Math-Science programs, with a rigorous, hands-on exploration of classical mechanics and data science. By utilizing the Gravitrax Pro modular system, students bridge the gap between abstract theoretical physics and tangible engineering application. The program scaffolds learning from fundamental Newtonian concepts‚Äîsuch as potential and kinetic energy‚Äîinto complex systems engineering, project management, and statistical quality control. This curriculum is engineered to foster college readiness by mirroring university-level laboratory environments where iterative design and quantitative analysis are paramount.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum aligns with the United States Department of Education (DOE) TRIO Upward Bound and UBMS program requirements as outlined in the Higher Education Act (HEA) Section 402C(b), specifically providing 'instruction in laboratory science' and 'instruction in mathematics.' Pursuant to 34 CFR Part 645.11, the activities provide required services in academic tutoring and instruction in core subjects. Furthermore, all associated equipment and field trip costs meet the 'necessary and reasonable' criteria for student support costs under EDGAR/2 CFR 200 Subpart E. The curriculum specifically targets the UBMS mandate for intensive STEM exposure, providing students with experience in data-driven research and exposure to STEM career pathways as required by HEA Sec. 402H.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is meticulously mapped to the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for Physics and Mathematics. Specifically, it addresses Physics standards 4(C) for two-dimensional motion, 6(B) for energy transformations, and 6(D) for conservation laws. Mathematical alignment includes Statistics standards 2(B) for experimental design and 4(A) for standard deviation. The course also integrates the Texas STEM Framework, emphasizing the Engineering Design Process (EDP), collaborative systems thinking, and technical communication, ensuring students meet the high rigor expected of college-bound scholars in the state of Texas.

Field Expeditions

To reinforce classroom concepts, the curriculum includes three strategic field trips within the Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex. Students will visit Six Flags Over Texas (Tarrant County) for 'Physics Day' to witness macro-scale applications of energy conservation and centripetal force. A visit to the Perot Museum of Nature and Science (Dallas County) provides deep-dive exploration into structural engineering and robotics within the Texas Instruments Innovation Hall. Finally, a tour of the University of Texas at Arlington (UTA) Civil Engineering Laboratories (Tarrant County) connects high school experiments to professional materials testing and collegiate research, offering essential campus exposure for TRIO participants.

Module Narratives

Introduction to Potential Energy & System Components

Students begin by conducting an inventory of mechanical components, classifying them by function. Through a controlled lab experiment, they calculate Gravitational Potential Energy (PEg) using precise measurements of mass and height, establishing the foundational physics required for complex builds.

Kinetic Energy & Conservation Principles

This module challenges students to apply the Law of Conservation of Energy. By comparing theoretical final velocity to measured velocity, students identify energy dissipation in the system, introducing them to the concept of efficiency and non-conservative forces.

Velocity Vectors & Slope Analysis

Utilizing trigonometry, students deconstruct gravity into vector components. By testing various slope angles, they graph the relationship between incline and acceleration, mirroring the analytical work required in civil and mechanical engineering.

Friction Fighters - Energy Loss

Focusing on tribology and mechanical efficiency, students experiment with track configurations to minimize rolling and air resistance. This activity emphasizes the iterative design process as students strive to maximize displacement through friction reduction.

The Gauss Cannon - Magnetic Acceleration

Students explore electromagnetism and momentum transfer through the use of magnetic accelerators. This lesson provides high-level instruction on how magnetic potential energy can be harvested to perform mechanical work, introducing concepts of particle acceleration.

Circular Motion & Loops

Focusing on centripetal force and inertia, students calculate the minimum velocity required for a marble to complete a vertical loop. This activity bridges the gap between classroom theory and real-world amusement park engineering.

Projectile Motion (The Jump)

Students analyze two-dimensional kinematics by designing track gaps. By separating horizontal and vertical motion vectors, they must predict landing coordinates, applying quadratic equations to hit specific targets.

The Hammer - Mechanical Advantage

This module introduces simple machines, specifically levers and torque. Students analyze the mechanical advantage provided by the 'Hammer' component, calculating how energy is stored and released through a fulcrum-based trigger.

Collisions & Momentum

Students investigate elastic and inelastic collisions by creating scenarios that demonstrate the conservation of momentum. They observe energy transfer between identical masses, providing a physical model for Newton‚Äôs third law.

Logic Gates & Switches

Connecting mechanics to computer science, students use mechanical switches to build binary logic paths. This activity introduces systems thinking and the 'if-then' logic used in automation and programming.

Statistical Analysis I - Data Collection

Shifting to quantitative research, students run 30 trials of a single track to collect a robust dataset. This module teaches the importance of sample size and the identification of experimental error in a laboratory setting.

Statistical Analysis II - Central Tendency & Spread

Students process their data from the previous module, calculating mean, median, and standard deviation. They create histograms to visualize the bell curve of their system's performance, learning how engineers ensure product reliability.

Engineering Design Process - The Constraints Challenge

Students are given a 'Budget' of limited parts and a specific time goal (e.g., exactly 10 seconds). This encourages creative problem-solving and the optimization of resources, a critical skill in industrial engineering.

Cost-Benefit Analysis (Simulated Budget)

Incorporating financial literacy, students are assigned costs for every component used. They must justify the 'purchase' of premium parts (like cannons) against the expected performance gains, mirroring corporate project management.

Collaborative Systems

Two independent teams must merge their tracks into a single functional system. This requires professional communication and the standardization of interfaces, simulating multi-departmental engineering projects.

Elevation Engineering

Focusing on statics and structural stability, students use vertical expansion components to build multi-story tracks. They must manage the center of gravity and column load-bearing to prevent structural failure.

Rube Goldberg Integration

Students are encouraged to innovate by integrating non-standard classroom objects into their builds. This promotes out-of-the-box thinking and tests their ability to transfer energy across different material types.

Final Capstone - Planning & Design

Teams begin their final project by creating detailed blueprints and project schedules. They must assign roles such as Project Manager and Architect, ensuring accountability and organizational structure.

Final Capstone - Construction & Testing

In the construction phase, teams execute their blueprints and conduct unit testing. This phase emphasizes troubleshooting, resilience, and the application of all physical and mathematical concepts learned throughout the course.

The Gravitrax Expo - Presentation

The curriculum concludes with a formal expo where students present their builds to peers and instructors. They must provide a technical defense of their design choices, showcasing their data, cost analysis, and mastery of physics principles.
