STEM Architect Master Guide

Illuminating Physics: Advanced Light, Color, and Shadow Analysis (Expanded)

This comprehensive 20-module curriculum utilizes professional-grade RGB LED studio lighting to explore high school physics concepts regarding geometric optics, the Inverse Square Law, additive color theory, and electromagnetic wave behavior. Designed for Upward Bound and UBMS programs, it transitions students from qualitative observation to quantitative analysis using app-controlled lighting technology, satisfying federal requirements for rigorous laboratory science instruction.

Target: High School (Ages 14-18) • Total Time: 800 minutes (20 activities, 40 minutes each) • Est. Cost: $175.00

Umbra and Penumbra: The Geometry of Shadows

Duration: 40 minutes TRIO: Both

Students explore the difference between point sources and extended light sources to understand shadow formation (umbra vs. penumbra) using similar triangles and geometric optics.

Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate between point sources and extended light sources.
  • Identify and measure the umbra and penumbra of a shadow.
  • Apply geometric similarity to calculate expected shadow dimensions.

Curriculum Narrative: Illuminating Physics: Advanced Light, Color, and Shadow Analysis (Expanded)

Executive Summary

The Illuminating Physics curriculum is a rigorous, 20-module instructional sequence designed specifically for Upward Bound and Upward Bound Math-Science (UB/UBMS) programs. By utilizing professional-grade, app-controlled RGB LED technology, this course transitions high school students from qualitative visual observation to complex quantitative analysis of electromagnetic radiation and geometric optics. Students engage in hands-on laboratory experiences that bridge the gap between abstract physics equations and real-world technological applications, such as fiber optics, solar energy efficiency, and digital imaging. The program is designed to foster a college-ready mindset by integrating algebraic and trigonometric modeling with experimental physics, satisfying the federal mandate for intensive laboratory science instruction while exposing participants to high-growth STEM career pathways.

Module Narratives

Umbra and Penumbra: The Geometry of Shadows

Students investigate the spatial relationship between light sources and objects by analyzing shadow formation. Using professional LED panels, they differentiate between point and extended sources, applying geometric similarity and similar triangles to predict and measure shadow dimensions, fostering essential spatial reasoning skills used in medical imaging and architectural design.

Fading Fast: Modeling the Inverse Square Law

This module introduces rigorous data collection where students use smartphone-based lux meters to track the decay of light intensity over distance. By performing regression analysis on their data, students mathematically verify the Inverse Square Law, a fundamental concept in both physics and radiation safety engineering.

Chromatic Shadows: Additive Color Physics

Integrating art with physics (STEAM), students utilize IoT-connected RGB lighting to explore additive color theory. They create complex colored shadows to understand how the human eye perceives white light and the overlap of primary wavelengths, a concept critical for careers in UI/UX design and cinematography.

The Law of Reflection: Plane Mirrors

Students engage in a foundational laboratory experiment to verify that the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection. By tracing narrow LED beams and constructing geometric normals, they build the baseline skills required for surveying and laser-based technical occupations.

Snell's Law: Refraction through Media

This advanced module requires students to apply trigonometry to physical phenomena. By measuring the 'bending' of light as it passes through acrylic blocks, students calculate the Index of Refraction, simulating the work of optical engineers and materials scientists.

Total Internal Reflection & Fiber Optics

Students explore the physics behind modern high-speed internet by determining the critical angle of light within a dense medium. This experiment demonstrates how information is transmitted via fiber optics, connecting physics principles to the telecommunications infrastructure.

Converging Lenses: Finding Focal Length

Using biconvex lenses, students create real, inverted images of LED arrays. They utilize the Thin Lens Equation to solve for focal length, mastering the algebraic manipulation of rational equations while exploring the technology used in cameras and telescopes.

Polarization: The Filter Effect

This activity demonstrates the wave nature of light by using polarizing filters to block transverse waves. Students learn how polarization is utilized in LCD screen technology and glare reduction, providing insight into the engineering of modern display systems.

Diffraction: Bending Light Around Corners

Students observe the interference patterns created when light passes through narrow slits or around hair strands. This module provides a conceptual bridge to quantum mechanics and crystallography, illustrating the wave-particle duality of light.

DIY Spectroscopy: Decoding Light

By constructing a diffraction-grating spectroscope, students analyze the discrete spectral output of various light sources. This activity links physics with chemical analysis, showing how light can be used to identify elements in stars or forensic samples.

Lambert's Cosine Law: Solar Simulation

Students simulate the efficiency of solar panels by varying the angle of light incidence and measuring intensity. This module applies trigonometric cosine functions to renewable energy challenges, preparing students for careers in environmental engineering.

The Blue Sky Effect: Rayleigh Scattering

Using a liquid medium and white LEDs, students replicate atmospheric scattering to explain why the sky appears blue and sunsets appear red. This laboratory bridges physics with meteorology and atmospheric science.

Color Rendering Index (CRI) Lab

Students evaluate the quality of light sources by comparing color fidelity under RGB-mixed white light versus broad-spectrum light. This introduces the professional metric of CRI, relevant to museum curation, interior design, and medical lighting.

The Pinhole Camera: Camera Obscura

Returning to the origins of optics, students build a camera obscura to witness the rectilinear propagation of light. They investigate how aperture size affects the trade-off between image brightness and sharpness, a fundamental concept in imaging science.

Chroma Key Physics: The Green Screen

Students explore the physics of digital sensors and the Bayer filter by investigating why green is the optimal color for background isolation. This connects wavelength sensitivity to the film and television production industry.

Mixing Pigment vs. Mixing Light

This comparative lab clarifies the distinction between additive and subtractive color theories. Students mix both paints and light beams to observe how the physics of absorption differs from emission, correcting a common scientific misconception.

Light Penetration and Absorption

Simulating deep-sea environments, students measure the attenuation of different wavelengths through water filters. This module introduces the exponential decay of energy and its implications for marine biology and underwater communication.

Anamorphic Art & Cylindrical Reflection

By exploring reflections off curved surfaces, students map coordinate transformations from Cartesian to polar planes. This engaging activity teaches the physics of distorted mirrors used in VR/AR correction and security mirrors.

Reciprocity: Photography Physics

Students use manual camera settings to investigate the 'Exposure Triangle.' They discover the proportional relationship between time (shutter speed) and intensity (light brightness), applying algebraic reasoning to the technology of digital imaging.

Capstone: Engineering a Lighting System

In this final challenge, students synthesize all curriculum concepts to design a lighting system for a specific technical brief. They must justify their equipment placement using the Inverse Square Law, reflection angles, and color theory, demonstrating mastery through engineering design.
