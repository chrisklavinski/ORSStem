Level Up: The Science and Strategy of Video Game Design

A comprehensive 10-module STEM curriculum designed for Texas TRIO programs (UB/UBMS). The curriculum aligns with Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) and prioritizes postsecondary readiness through specific career and college major exploration.

Curriculum Narrative: Level Up ‚Äì Integrating STEM Rigor through Video Game Design and Strategic Analysis

Executive Summary

Level Up: The Science and Strategy of Video Game Design is a comprehensive 10-module STEM curriculum specifically engineered for High School students (Grades 9-12) within Texas TRIO Upward Bound and Upward Bound Math-Science programs. By leveraging the high-engagement domain of video game development, the curriculum delivers rigorous instruction in Algebra II, Physics, Computer Science, and Biology. Each module is designed to bridge the gap between abstract academic concepts and concrete professional applications, prioritizing postsecondary readiness through structured career exploration and college major alignment. The program encompasses 400 minutes of intensive classroom instruction supplemented by targeted field experiences, ensuring students develop the cognitive and technical competencies required for success in competitive STEM degree programs.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is meticulously designed to satisfy the legislative requirements for DOE TRIO Upward Bound (UB) and Upward Bound Math-Science (UBMS) programs. In accordance with the Higher Education Act (HEA) Section 402C(b), the program provides mandatory instruction in mathematics through Algebra II and Statistics, and in laboratory science through Physics and Biology modules. The curriculum complies with Title 34 CFR Part 645.11(a)(1), (b)(1), and (a)(4), addressing the mandates for academic instruction, increased rigor in math/science, and career awareness. Financial and administrative implementation follows EDGAR and 2 CFR 200.453 cost principles, ensuring that all proposed materials (e.g., graphing calculators, lab supplies, and instructional data sets) are necessary and reasonable for the performance of the federal award objectives, specifically the improvement of secondary school retention and postsecondary enrollment rates.

Standards Alignment

The Level Up curriculum is rigorously aligned with the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) and the Texas STEM Framework. Academic benchmarks include ¬ß111.43 (Algebra II) for exponential functions and quadratic modeling, ¬ß112.39 (Physics) for kinematics and wave characteristics, and ¬ß130.422 (Computer Science I) for hardware analysis and algorithmic logic. The curriculum utilizes the 'STEM Process' standards by requiring students to engage in data analysis, scientific inquiry, and computational thinking. By mapping directly to Texas's high school graduation endorsements in STEM, the curriculum ensures that students are not only meeting state graduation requirements but are also developing the specialized skills required by the Texas workforce in Information Technology and Advanced Engineering.

Field Expeditions

The curriculum includes three high-impact field trip options within a 60-mile radius of Dallas, Tarrant, and Johnson counties, providing real-world context to theoretical instruction. Students visit the National Videogame Museum in Frisco (Collin County) to analyze the historical evolution of hardware and software engineering. A visit to the University of Texas at Dallas (UTD) Computer Science Department offers direct exposure to a Tier One research institution and specific 'Animation and Games' degree concentrations, facilitating the 'college going' culture required by TRIO. Finally, participation in Physics Day at Six Flags Over Texas in Arlington (Tarrant County) allows students to utilize accelerometers and timing devices to validate the Newtonian physics and projectile motion equations mastered during the classroom modules.

Module Narratives

Module 1: The Silicon Heart - Hardware & History

Students engage in a high-rigor analysis of computing evolution by investigating Moore's Law. Through graphing transistor density against historical timelines, students apply exponential functions to understand the physical constraints of hardware, bridging the gap between electrical engineering and software performance.

Module 2: The Loot Box Logic - Probability

This module demystifies complex statistics through the lens of game economy. Students simulate 'drop rates' using dice and spreadsheets, calculating theoretical versus experimental probability to master expected value and random number generation (RNG), skills directly transferable to data science and actuarial careers.

Module 3: Physics Engines - Projectile Motion

Focusing on advanced kinematics, students calculate trajectories and velocity vectors used in modern physics engines. By applying quadratic equations and trigonometry to determine flight paths, students see the immediate relevance of calculus and physics in software simulation and mechanical engineering.

Module 4: Algorithmic Thinking - The 'Human' Program

Students develop computational thinking by creating complex logic flowcharts. By 'programming' classmates to navigate obstacles, they learn to identify and debug logic errors, establishing a foundational understanding of Boolean algebra and the systems engineering mindset required for professional software development.

Module 5: Xenobiology - Designing for Biomes

Integrating life sciences, this module requires students to apply principles of adaptation, homeostasis, and natural selection. Students design creatures for extreme environments, justifying their biological features through a scientific lens that connects evolutionary biology with character design and astrobiology.

Module 6: Level Design Geometry

Using the Cartesian coordinate system, students design balanced game maps. They utilize the distance formula and area calculations of composite shapes to ensure spatial symmetry, providing a practical application for geometry and civil engineering concepts in a digital environment.

Module 7: The Physics of Sound (Foley)

Students explore the mechanical properties of sound waves by creating 'Foley' effects. By analyzing frequency and amplitude while manipulating physical objects to create digital audio, students gain a deep understanding of acoustics and wave physics relevant to audio engineering and material science.

Module 8: The Human Controller - Reaction Time

This lab-based module explores the intersection of biology and physics. Students measure neural latency through physical tests, using kinematics formulas to convert distance into time, thereby understanding the biological pathways of the nervous system and the technical constraints of input lag.

Module 9: Gaming Economics - Inflation & Currency

Students analyze virtual markets to understand macroeconomics. By graphing supply and demand curves and calculating the impact of currency devaluation within a game, they master financial literacy concepts like inflation and purchasing power, preparing them for degrees in finance or economics.

Module 10: Press Start on Career - Pathways

The capstone module facilitates direct postsecondary planning. Students map their personal interests to specific STEM career roles, research Texas-based university degree requirements, and draft a four-year high school academic roadmap to ensure they meet the prerequisites for competitive college admissions.
