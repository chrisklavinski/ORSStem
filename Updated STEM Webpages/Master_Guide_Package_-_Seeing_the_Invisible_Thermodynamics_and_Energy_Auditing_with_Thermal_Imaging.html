Module 1: The Electromagnetic Spectrum & Thermal Visualization

Duration: 90 Minutes TRIO: Both

Students are introduced to the Bosch GTC400C to visualize the invisible. They will explore the difference between visible light and infrared radiation through a series of 'mystery item' temperature challenges.

Learning Objectives

  • Define the electromagnetic spectrum and locate Infrared (IR) radiation within it.
  • Demonstrate proper operation and calibration of the Bosch GTC400C.
  • Differentiate between temperature (intensity) and heat (transfer of energy).

Materials

  • Bosch GTC400C Thermal Camera (1 per group of 3-4)$399.00
  • Ice cubes and warm water$5.00
  • Black plastic trash bags (opaque to visible light, transparent to IR)$10.00
  • Various materials (aluminum foil, wood, brick samples)$0.00 (Scrap)

Teacher Guide

1.

Step 1: Introduction to Light (15 mins) - Project an image of the EM spectrum. Ask students what 'Heat' looks like. Explain that thermal cameras translate IR radiation (invisible) into visible colors.

2.

Step 2: The Magic Trick (10 mins) - Hold a black trash bag up. Ask if students can see through it. Put your hand behind it. Ask again. Turn on the Thermal Camera connected to the projector/screen. Show that IR passes through the plastic even though visible light does not. This is the 'Hook'.

3.

Step 3: Equipment Safety & Setup (10 mins) - Distribute Bosch GTC400C units. Strict rule: NEVER point the thermal sensor at the sun or high-power lasers (it damages the sensor). Show how to flip the cover and insert the 12V battery.

4.

Step 4: Exploration Station (30 mins) - Have stations set up: Station A (Ice water vs Hot water), Station B (Friction - rub hands together then touch table), Station C (Reflections - look at a shiny metal sheet vs wood). Discussion Prompt: 'Why does the metal reflection look 'cold' even if the metal is room temp?' (Answer: It is reflecting the cold room temperature, low emissivity).

5.

Step 5: Analysis (15 mins) - Have students use the 'Freeze' button to save their best image. Connect via USB or Wifi to download one image per group.

6.

Step 6: Debrief (10 mins) - Check for understanding: 'Does the camera see temperature or radiation?' (Radiation). Troubleshooting: If images are blurry, ensure they are at least 1 meter away from the target (fixed focus limitations).

Module 2: The Energy Audit (Engineering & Environmental Science)

Duration: 120 Minutes TRIO: UBMS

Students act as 'Energy Auditors' to inspect the school building or classroom. They will identify thermal bridges (insulation gaps), air leaks around windows, and HVAC efficiency.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify modes of heat transfer (Conduction, Convection, Radiation) in a real-world setting.
  • Calculate Delta T (ŒîT) to determine the severity of energy loss.
  • Propose engineering solutions to improve building energy efficiency.

Materials

  • Bosch GTC400C Thermal Camera(Reused)
  • Blue painters tape (to mark measurement spots)$6.00
  • Clipboards and Audit Sheets (printed)$2.00
  • Hairdryer (to simulate heat leak if weather is mild)$20.00

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Exposure to Engineering and Architecture careers., Instruction in Mathematics (Delta T calculation, data analysis).
Citations
HEA Sec. 402C(b)(1): Academic instruction in mathematics and science., HEA Sec. 402C(b)(4): Guidance on secondary school reentry or alternative education (Vocational pathways)., 34 CFR 645.11(a)(1): Academic instruction..., 34 CFR 645.11(b): Allowable services - Career awareness., 2 CFR 200.453: Materials and supplies., 2 CFR 200.461: Publication and printing costs.

Teacher Guide

1.

Step 1: Theory - Heat Transfer (20 mins) - Use the whiteboard. Draw a wall section. Explain 'Thermal Bridging' (where studs conduct heat faster than insulation). Explain that energy moves from High to Low temperature. If it's hot outside and cool inside, we look for HEAT coming in.

2.

Step 2: The Mission (10 mins) - Tell students they are now 'Energy Engineers.' Their job is to save the school money on electricity. Hand out the 'Audit Log'.

3.

Step 3: Zone Assignments (5 mins) - Divide class. Group A: Windows. Group B: HVAC Vents/Ceiling. Group C: Electrical Outlets/Exterior Walls. Group D: Doors.

4.

Step 4: The Sweep (45 mins) - Walk with students. Guide them to look for color gradients. A sharp line usually means a draft or lack of insulation. Ask: 'Is that blue spot a water leak or just cold air?' (Evaporation causes cooling). Troubleshooting: If the room is isothermal (same temp everywhere), use the hairdryer to heat a spot on the wall to simulate a 'hot day' exterior for demonstration.

5.

Step 5: Data Analysis (20 mins) - Teams calculate the ŒîT for their worst spot. (Spot Temp - Room Ambient Temp).

6.

Step 6: Engineering Proposal (20 mins) - Each group presents one 'fix' (e.g., 'We need weather stripping on the North door').

Module 3: Circuit Safety & Electrical Inspection

Duration: 60 Minutes TRIO: UB

Students explore the relationship between electrical resistance and heat. They will safely inspect low-voltage circuits or power strips to identify 'overloaded' components using thermal imaging.

Learning Objectives

  • Correlate electrical resistance and current flow with heat generation (Joule Heating).
  • Identify potential electrical hazards (overheating components) using non-contact tools.
  • Understand the role of predictive maintenance in industry.

Materials

  • Bosch GTC400C Thermal Camera(Reused)
  • Power strip with multiple chargers plugged in (Phone, Laptop)$0.00 (Bring from home)
  • Incandescent bulb vs LED bulb lamp setup$15.00

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Mentoring programs involving elementary or secondary school teachers or faculty members at institutions of higher education., Workshops and counseling services on career options.
Citations
HEA Sec. 402C(b)(1): Academic instruction., HEA Sec. 402C(b)(4): Guidance on... alternative education programs (Vocational)., 34 CFR 645.11(a)(1): Academic instruction in... laboratory science., 34 CFR 645.11(b)(1): Career interest inventories and workshops., 2 CFR 200.453: Supplies., 2 CFR 200.405: Allocable costs.

Teacher Guide

1.

Step 1: Safety First (5 mins) - Emphasize that thermal cameras allow us to inspect dangerous high-voltage equipment from a SAFE distance. We never open electrical panels, we look at the surface.

2.

Step 2: Physics Concept (10 mins) - Introduce Joule Heating ($P = I^2R$). Explain that as electrons bump into atoms in a wire, they create heat. Loose connections create MORE resistance, which creates MORE heat, which causes fires.

3.

Step 3: The Light Bulb Challenge (15 mins) - Turn on an LED lamp and an Incandescent lamp. Wait 2 minutes. Ask students to predict which is hotter. Use the camera to verify. Discuss efficiency (Heat = Wasted Energy in a lightbulb).

4.

Step 4: The Load Test (20 mins) - Plug a laptop charger and a phone charger into a power strip. Let them run for a while. Have students scan the power strip. Ask: 'Why is the transformer block hot?' (Voltage conversion creates heat).

5.

Step 5: Career Connection (10 mins) - Explain that factories pay people to walk around with these cameras to find motors or breakers that are about to explode. This is called 'Predictive Maintenance'.

Seeing the Invisible: A Comprehensive Narrative on Thermodynamics and Energy Auditing with Thermal Imaging for UBMS Programs

Executive Summary

This curriculum, titled 'Seeing the Invisible: Thermodynamics and Energy Auditing with Thermal Imaging,' leverages the advanced Bosch GTC400C Thermal Camera to transform abstract thermodynamic principles into tangible, visual learning experiences. Targeted at high school students in Upward Bound (UB) and Upward Bound Math-Science (UBMS) programs, the course moves beyond theoretical physics to immerse students in real-world applications such as building energy audits, electrical system inspections, and material science. By bridging the gap between classroom science and high-demand STEM careers in engineering and environmental consulting, this program provides rigorous academic preparation and technical skill development necessary for postsecondary success.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This program is specifically designed to comply with the Higher Education Act (HEA) Section 402C(b)(1), which mandates academic instruction in laboratory science for TRIO projects. It aligns with 34 CFR 645.11(a)(1) by providing direct instructional services in physics and environmental science, and 34 CFR 645.11(b)(1) through career awareness workshops focused on engineering and thermography. Under the Uniform Guidance 2 CFR 200.453 and 2 CFR 200.403 (EDGAR), the acquisition of Bosch GTC400C thermal imaging units is categorized as a necessary and reasonable cost, as these tools are essential specialized equipment for the delivery of the laboratory-based STEM curriculum and are used directly for student instruction in data processing and analysis.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is meticulously mapped to the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for Physics, specifically sections 7.A (Electromagnetic Spectrum), 7.B (Wave Behavior), and 6.A (Thermal Energy). It also addresses Environmental Systems TEKS 5.B regarding energy conservation methods and Engineering Design & Problem Solving TEKS 5.A. Furthermore, the modules align with the Texas STEM Framework by requiring students to apply the scientific process (Science 1.A) and utilize digital technology tools to analyze complex data sets (Technology 4.A), ensuring students meet the high rigor expected in Texas secondary education.

Field Expeditions

The program includes strategic field trip options within Dallas, Tarrant, and Johnson counties to reinforce classroom learning through site-specific observation. Students may visit the Perot Museum of Nature and Science (Dallas) to explore sensor technology, the LEED-certified Botanical Research Institute of Texas (Fort Worth) to witness sustainable architecture in action, or the University of Texas at Arlington (UTA) Civil Engineering Labs to see industrial material testing. Additionally, visits to the Oncor Training Center or Dallas College‚Äôs Construction Management Center provide direct exposure to the industrial applications of thermography in electrical grid maintenance and HVAC diagnostics, offering high-value career orientation within 60 miles of the target service area.

Module Narratives

The Electromagnetic Spectrum & Thermal Visualization

This introductory module utilizes the Bosch GTC400C to make the invisible visible, challenging students to differentiate between visible light and infrared radiation. Through mystery-object investigations and friction-based heat signatures, students engage in high-rigor scientific observation while developing technical proficiency with professional-grade imaging hardware, directly mirroring the workflows used in photonics and optical engineering careers.

The Energy Audit: Engineering & Environmental Science

Students transition into the role of Energy Engineers, conducting an intensive audit of their physical environment to identify thermal bridges and air leaks. This module emphasizes the engineering design process and mathematical rigor as students calculate Delta T (temperature difference) to quantify energy loss. By proposing evidence-based solutions for building efficiency, students gain practical experience in civil engineering and environmental sustainability.

Circuit Safety & Electrical Inspection

Exploring the intersection of electricity and thermodynamics, this module introduces students to Joule Heating and the physics of electrical resistance. Students use thermal imaging for non-contact inspection of low-voltage circuits, identifying 'hot spots' that signify inefficiency or potential failure. This activity highlights the importance of predictive maintenance and safety engineering, connecting classroom physics to high-stakes industrial and electrical technology career pathways.
