The Chemistry and Physics of Paint: From Pigments to Polymers

This curriculum guides High School students through the chemical composition of paints, the mathematics of color separation, and the engineering of functional conductive art. Students will move beyond art as an aesthetic practice to understand the molecular interactions of binders, solvents, and pigments, as well as the physics of electricity through conductive materials. The curriculum is designed to meet Texas state standards while fulfilling Federal TRIO grant objectives for rigorous STEM instruction.

Target: High School (Ages 14-18) • Total Time: 120 Minutes (Three 40-minute modules) • Est. Cost: $130.50

Module 1: Deconstructing Paint - The Chemistry of Binders

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: Both

Students investigate the chemical composition of paint by synthesizing their own paints using different ancient and modern binders (egg yolk, casein/milk, and PVA glue). They will analyze how different polymers suspend pigments and adhere to surfaces.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the three main components of paint: pigment, binder, and solvent.
  • Compare the chemical properties of protein-based binders (egg, milk) versus synthetic polymer binders (acrylic/glue).
  • Observe and describe the properties of emulsions and colloids.

Teacher Guide

1.

Preparation: Separate 2-3 egg yolks and place in cups. Prepare a small amount of milk mixed with vinegar to create curdles (casein) beforehand as a demo.

2.

Introduction (5 mins): Discuss that paint is not just 'color'. Introduce the formula: Paint = Pigment (Color) + Binder (Glue) + Vehicle (Thinner). Discuss historical context (Cave paintings used blood/fat; Renaissance used egg).

3.

Demonstration (5 mins): Show raw pigment powder. Blow on it (gently) to show it doesn't stick to paper. Explain the need for a polymer/binder to lock it to the substrate.

4.

Activity (25 mins): Distribute materials. Have students mix the same pigment (e.g., Red) with three different binders: Egg Yolk (Tempera), PVA Glue (Acrylic-like), and Water (control).

5.

Observation (5 mins): Students apply all three to paper. Discuss texture, drying time, and sheen. Discuss why the water sample fails (evaporation leaves only loose powder).

2

Module 2: Chromatography Forensics - The Math of Mixtures

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: Both

Using water-soluble black markers and paints, students perform paper chromatography to calculate the Retention Factor (Rf) of different pigment components. This connects the physical separation of mixtures to mathematical ratios.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the principles of chromatography as a separation technique.
  • Calculate Retention Factor (Rf) values using division and measurement skills.
  • Analyze how polarity affects the movement of molecules through a medium.

Materials

  • Chromatography paper (or coffee filters)$8.00
  • Various black markers (water-soluble) and liquid watercolors$10.00
  • Beakers or clear plastic cups$12.00
  • Rulers (metric)$5.00
  • Pencils$2.00

Teacher Guide

1.

Intro (5 mins): Explain that 'black' is often a mixture of many colors. Introduce the concept of Polarity‚Äîwater pulls polar pigments further up the paper.

2.

Setup (5 mins): Distribute strips of filter paper. Ensure students use PENCIL for the starting line (graphite doesn't dissolve). Markers will ruin the experiment if used for the start line.

3.

Experiment (15 mins): Students dot different black inks/paints on the start line and suspend the paper in water. Vital: The water level must be BELOW the ink dots, or the ink will wash into the cup.

4.

Analysis (15 mins): Once the solvent front reaches near the top, remove and dry. Teach the Rf calculation: Distance traveled by pigment / Distance traveled by water.

5.

Discussion: Why did some colors travel further? (Higher solubility/polarity).

3

Module 3: Electric Art - Conductive Paint Engineering

Duration: 40 Minutes TRIO: Both

Students bridge art and engineering by using conductive paint (graphite or carbon-based) to create functioning electrical circuits on paper. They will design a painting that lights up an LED, learning about resistance, continuity, and voltage.

Learning Objectives

  • Construct a complete series circuit using non-traditional conductors (paint).
  • Explain the relationship between paint thickness/length and electrical resistance.
  • Integrate aesthetic design with functional engineering constraints.

Materials

  • Conductive Paint Pens (or graphite sticks/soft pencils 6B-9B)$30.00
  • 3V Coin Cell Batteries$8.00
  • 5mm LEDs (various colors)$5.00
  • Heavy cardstock paper$5.00
  • Binder clips or scotch tape$3.00

Federal Compliance

Allowable Services
Exposure to engineering or technology, Instruction in laboratory science
Citations
Section 402C(b) (UB): Exposure to new technologies, Section 402E(b) (UBMS): Exposure to engineering, 34 CFR ¬ß 645.11(b)(3) (Exposure to careers in which disadvantaged persons are underrepresented), 34 CFR ¬ß 645.11(a)(1) (Science instruction), 2 CFR ¬ß 200.453 (Materials and supplies)

Teacher Guide

1.

Intro (5 mins): Review simple circuits (Power source, Path, Load). Introduce conductive paint as a 'liquid wire' that has higher resistance than copper.

2.

Challenge (5 mins): Sketch a design where the lines of the drawing serve as the wires for the circuit.

3.

Fabrication (20 mins): Students draw thick, continuous lines with conductive paint/pencils. They must leave a gap for the LED and a gap for the battery. Emphasize that the paint must be wet/thick for good conductivity.

4.

Testing (10 mins): Attach the LED (long leg to positive) and battery. Troubleshoot. If the light is dim, the line is too long or thin (High Resistance).

5.

Wrap up: Discuss how the material properties of the paint affect electron flow compared to standard wires.

The Chemistry and Physics of Paint: From Pigments to Polymers - A STEM Integration Curriculum

Executive Summary

This interdisciplinary curriculum, 'The Chemistry and Physics of Paint: From Pigments to Polymers,' is designed to engage high school students in a rigorous exploration of material science, chemical engineering, and applied physics. By deconstructing paint into its fundamental chemical components‚Äîpigments, binders, and solvents‚Äîstudents move beyond the aesthetic to the analytic. The program integrates three comprehensive modules that cover the synthesis of polymers, the mathematical precision of chromatography, and the engineering of conductive circuitry. This hands-on approach is designed to foster critical thinking and provide a gateway to high-demand STEM career pathways such as analytical chemistry, conservation science, and electrical engineering, specifically tailored for students within federal college-access programs.

The program serves the federal objective of improving the academic performance of participants by providing high-quality, college-preparatory STEM experiences.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is meticulously aligned with the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for high school science and mathematics. Key alignments include ¬ß112.35 (Chemistry) regarding solubility rules and physical versus chemical changes, ¬ß112.39 (Physics) concerning the design and construction of electrical circuits, and ¬ß111.39 (Algebra I) regarding mathematical process standards and ratio calculations. Furthermore, the curriculum supports the Texas STEM Framework by prioritizing inquiry-based learning, the application of the engineering design process, and the demonstration of mastery in core scientific concepts through real-world applications.

Field Expeditions

Educational enrichment is supported by site visits within the Dallas-Fort Worth-Cleburne metroplex. Students may visit the Kimbell Art Museum in Fort Worth (Tarrant County) for a tour of the Conservation Department, providing insight into the material science of historical pigments. Alternatively, the Perot Museum of Nature and Science in Dallas (Dallas County) offers exploration of the Lyda Hill Gems and Minerals Hall, allowing students to trace the geological origins of inorganic pigments. These trips, located within 60 miles of Dallas, Tarrant, and Johnson counties, provide high-impact exposure to careers in museum curation and chemical analysis, reinforcing the academic concepts explored in the modules.

Module Narratives

Module 1: Deconstructing Paint - The Chemistry of Binders

In this module, students act as material scientists, synthesizing various paint types by testing the chemical properties of ancient protein-based binders like casein and egg yolk against modern synthetic polymers. This activity emphasizes the rigor of molecular interactions, teaching students to distinguish between physical mixtures and chemical emulsions. By investigating the adherence and durability of their own formulations, students gain insight into career paths in chemical engineering and art conservation while mastering TEKS-aligned concepts of solubility and colloids.

Module 2: Chromatography Forensics - The Math of Mixtures

This activity transforms the classroom into a forensic laboratory where students use paper chromatography to separate complex pigment mixtures. The focus is on the mathematical application of the Retention Factor (Rf), requiring students to use metric measurements and algebraic division to derive data-driven conclusions. This module reinforces the concept of polarity and molecular mass, providing a concrete example of how analytical chemistry is used in environmental testing and criminal investigations.

Module 3: Electric Art - Conductive Paint Engineering

Bridging the gap between fine art and electrical engineering, students utilize conductive materials to design and troubleshoot functional paper circuits. This module challenges students to apply Ohm's Law and the principles of resistance and continuity to create interactive 'Electric Art.' The activity emphasizes the engineering design process‚Äîplanning, prototyping, and debugging‚Äîwhile exposing students to the emerging field of flexible electronics and wearable technology, preparing them for future postsecondary pursuits in applied physics and industrial design.
