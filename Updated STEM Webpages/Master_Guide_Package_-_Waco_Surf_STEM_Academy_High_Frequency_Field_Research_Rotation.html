Waco Surf STEM Academy: High-Frequency Field Research Rotation

A refined, high-intensity rotation curriculum designed for UB/UBMS students in Texas (Dallas, Tarrant, Johnson counties). This curriculum breaks down complex hydrodynamics and environmental engineering concepts into 20 distinct, rapid-fire micro-modules (sprints). Students rotate through stations at the Waco Surf facility, covering Physics, Chemistry, Mathematics, and Engineering in 20-30 minute intervals. The curriculum is strictly aligned with HEA Title IV regulations for allowable costs and services, providing rigorous academic instruction in a field setting.

Waco Surf STEM Academy: High-Frequency Field Research Rotation ‚Äì Comprehensive Curriculum Narrative

Executive Summary

The Waco Surf STEM Academy: High-Frequency Field Research Rotation is a rigorous, field-based academic program designed for High School students (Ages 14-18) within the TRIO Upward Bound and Upward Bound Math-Science (UBMS) framework. This curriculum utilizes the unique, controlled-environment pneumatic wave technology at the Waco Surf facility to deliver high-intensity instruction across twenty distinct micro-modules. By rotating through rapid-fire laboratory stations, students engage in hands-on data collection and analysis regarding hydrodynamics, fluid mechanics, environmental chemistry, and structural engineering. The curriculum transitions students from theoretical classroom concepts to professional-grade field research, emphasizing the application of the scientific method in a real-world, industrial-recreational setting.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is meticulously aligned with the Higher Education Act (HEA) of 1965, specifically Sec. 402C(b) for Upward Bound, which mandates instruction in mathematics and science, and Sec. 402E(b) for UBMS, which requires exposure to scientific research. In accordance with 34 CFR 645.11(a)(1), these modules provide 'academic instruction' through structured field laboratory exercises. The associated field trip costs are allowable under 34 CFR 645.40(c), provided they are part of a 'curriculum-related' activity. Furthermore, all expenditures for instructional supplies and transportation comply with EDGAR/2 CFR 200 Subpart E cost principles, specifically 2 CFR 200.403 (necessary and reasonable), 2 CFR 200.404 (reasonable costs), and 2 CFR 200.405 (allocable costs to the federal award), ensuring that the program's financial footprint remains within strict federal guidelines for TRIO projects.

Standards Alignment

The Waco Surf STEM Academy curriculum is strictly mapped to the Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) and the Texas STEM Framework. Primary alignments include Physics 7(B) and 7(D) regarding wave characteristics and behaviors; Chemistry 10(H) and 11(A) regarding acid-base reactions and water's unique chemical roles; and Geometry 11(D) regarding volume formulas and dimensional analysis. Students progress through STEM Framework Levels 2 through 5, moving from basic tool usage and data collection to complex synthesis and peer-reviewed communication of research findings, ensuring that the experience prepares them for post-secondary academic rigor.

Field Expeditions

The primary field laboratory for this curriculum is the Waco Surf Artificial Wave Facility. While situated just outside the immediate 60-mile radius of the core Dallas/Tarrant/Johnson county area, it serves as the nearest 'controlled environment' hydrodynamics lab capable of producing repeatable pneumatic waves for scientific study. Unlike natural bodies of water within the DFW metroplex, this facility allows students to isolate variables such as frequency, amplitude, and bathymetric slope. The educational value lies in its industrial-scale filtration and power systems, which provide a unique venue for studying mechanical engineering and environmental systems that cannot be replicated in a standard classroom or municipal park setting.

Module Narratives

Wave Period Analysis

Students engage in high-precision timing of pneumatic wave sets to calculate frequency and period. This module emphasizes the mathematical relationship between time and oscillatory motion, requiring students to mitigate human error through statistical averaging, directly mirroring career tasks in signal processing and oceanography.

Amplitude Measurement

Utilizing visual triangulation and metric wall markers, students measure the vertical displacement of waves from equilibrium. This exercise differentiates between wave height and amplitude, requiring spatial reasoning and precision measurement relevant to coastal defense engineering.

Velocity Calculation

Students apply kinematics formulas (v=d/t) to track the speed of energy pulses across the lagoon. This module challenges students to differentiate between the movement of medium particles and energy propagation, a fundamental concept in advanced physics.

Wave Decay & Friction

In this advanced module, students analyze energy loss as waves transition from deep water to the shoreline. By calculating percentage decrease, students investigate the Law of Conservation of Energy and the impact of benthic friction on hydrodynamic efficiency.

Wavelength Estimation

Students use scaling and reference points to estimate spatial frequency. This module connects visual observation to algebraic verification, as students use the wave equation (v = f * lambda) to check the accuracy of their field estimations.

Bathymetry Slope Math

Participants perform a basic surveying exercise to determine the 'Rise over Run' of the beach entry. This application of trigonometry illustrates how seafloor topography determines the mechanics of breaking waves, a key competency for civil engineers and surveyors.

Total Volume Estimation

Students decompose the complex geometry of the lagoon into manageable geometric solids. Using dimensional analysis, they convert cubic meters to gallons, simulating the critical calculations required for industrial water management and reservoir planning.

Flow Rate Analysis

By measuring output at filtration return jets, students calculate Gallons Per Minute (GPM) and turnover rates. This module introduces students to mechanical systems and the logistics of maintaining large-scale aquatic environments.

pH Field Test

This chemistry-focused station teaches students to use colorimetric strips and digital sensors to monitor water acidity. Students explore the logarithmic nature of the pH scale and its implications for public health and bather safety.

Chlorine & UV Interaction

Students investigate photolysis by comparing chemical concentrations in sun-exposed versus shaded areas. This experiment highlights the role of UV radiation in chemical bonding and the environmental factors affecting water treatment.

Thermal Stratification

Using digital probe thermometers, students map the thermocline of the lagoon. This activity explores the density of water and the thermodynamics of heat transfer within a large fluid system.

Turbidity Assessment

Students utilize a Secchi disk to measure light penetration and water clarity. This module introduces the concept of Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) and the environmental engineering required to maintain clarity in high-load facilities.

Drag Test - Surface Area

Using spring scales, students measure the force required to pull objects of varying surface areas through the water. This provides a tangible demonstration of fluid resistance and the proportionality of force to area.

Drag Test - Hydrodynamics

This module compares the drag coefficients of blocky versus streamlined shapes. Students observe turbulent flow and eddies, gaining insight into the design principles used in naval architecture and automotive engineering.

Buoyancy & Density

Students apply Archimedes' Principle by testing the buoyancy of various material cubes. The module explores the relationship between mass, volume, and displacement, providing a foundational understanding of maritime logistics.

Pneumatic Sound Analysis

Utilizing decibel meters, students analyze the acoustic footprint of the wave generator. This introduces concepts of industrial safety, acoustics, and the inverse square law of sound propagation.

Pump Station Power Estimation

Students read industrial motor specifications to calculate kilowatt-hour usage and operational costs. This bridge between physics and economics highlights the energy demands of large-scale mechanical systems.

Concrete & Material Science

Through tactile observation and magnification, students examine the properties of pool liners and polymers. They discuss friction coefficients and material durability, introducing the field of materials science and architecture.

Physiological Response

Students use pulse oximeters to track heart rate changes before and after physical exertion in the water. This biology-centered station explores homeostasis and the human body's systemic response to environmental stressors.

Data Symposium & Career Synthesis

In the final module, students aggregate their findings and participate in a peer-review session. They identify specific STEM career pathways observed throughout the day, synthesizing their field data into a cohesive understanding of facility operations and professional research.
