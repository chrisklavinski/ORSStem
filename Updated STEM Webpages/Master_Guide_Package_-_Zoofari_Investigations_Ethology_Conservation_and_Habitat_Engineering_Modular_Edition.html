Zoofari Investigations: Ethology, Conservation, and Habitat Engineering (Modular Edition)

This refined STEM curriculum adapts the scientific study of wildlife into highly flexible 30-minute modules. It transforms a visit to Fossil Rim Wildlife Center into a rigorous scientific field study suitable for UB and UBMS grants. To accommodate tight schedules and precise compliance reporting, the Field Study component has been segmented into specific 30-minute academic data-collection modules.

Notes & Observations

Curriculum Narrative: Zoofari Investigations: Ethology, Conservation, and Habitat Engineering (Modular Edition)

Executive Summary

Zoofari Investigations is a rigorous, 14-hour modular STEM curriculum designed to bridge the gap between high school biological sciences and collegiate-level field research. By utilizing Fossil Rim Wildlife Center as a living laboratory, students engage in ethology‚Äîthe scientific study of animal behavior‚Äîthrough systematic data collection and analysis. The curriculum is structured into 30-minute instructional segments to provide maximum flexibility for Upward Bound (UB) and Upward Bound Math-Science (UBMS) programs. Students progress from foundational vocabulary and behavioral categorization to advanced statistical analysis and engineering design, ultimately prototyping enrichment solutions that address real-world conservation challenges. This multidisciplinary approach fosters critical thinking, quantitative literacy, and a direct pipeline to careers in veterinary medicine, wildlife biology, and environmental engineering.

Compliance & Alignment

DOE TRIO Compliance Strategy

This curriculum is meticulously engineered to meet the requirements of the Higher Education Act (HEA) of 1965, specifically Section 402C(b)(1) regarding mandatory academic instruction in mathematics and laboratory science for UB/UBMS projects. It adheres to 34 CFR Part 645.11(b)(1), providing rigorous instruction to improve the academic performance of students in the core area of science. Field study components are aligned with 34 CFR 645.11(b)(5), offering exposure to academic programs and career sites. All associated costs, including admission to the Fossil Rim Wildlife Center and instructional materials, are justified under the cost principles of EDGAR and 2 CFR 200.403-405, qualifying as necessary, reasonable, and allocable. Furthermore, field trip expenditures are specifically cited as allowable under 2 CFR 200.432 for educational purposes that directly support the program's academic objectives.

Standards Alignment

The curriculum is fully aligned with Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS) for Biology (¬ß112.34), Environmental Systems (¬ß112.37), and Physics (¬ß112.39), with a specific emphasis on scientific inquiry and the application of mathematical models to biological data. It integrates the Texas STEM Framework by prioritizing STEM Content Standards 1.C (Engineering Design) and 3.A (Scientific Inquiry), ensuring students develop the technical competencies required by the Texas Education Agency for college readiness in STEM fields.

Field Expeditions

The cornerstone of this curriculum is a field study at the Fossil Rim Wildlife Center in Glen Rose, TX. Situated within 60 miles of Tarrant and Johnson counties, and easily accessible from Dallas County, this 1,800-acre facility provides an unparalleled environment for students to observe endangered species in semi-natural habitats. Unlike traditional zoo visits, this experience is structured as a scientific expedition where students act as field researchers. The facility‚Äôs focus on conservation and rangeland management provides a direct application for ethological sampling, offering high educational value through immersion in professional wildlife management practices.

Module Narratives

Module 1.1: Introduction to Ethology

This foundational module introduces students to the formal discipline of ethology, moving beyond casual animal observation to scientific inquiry. Students differentiate between innate and learned behaviors, establishing the cognitive framework necessary for professional animal science careers while building essential vocabulary for subsequent field research.

Module 1.2: Objectivity & Anthropomorphism

Academic rigor is emphasized as students learn to strip subjective bias from their scientific reporting. By identifying and eliminating anthropomorphism, students develop the high-level writing skills required for technical science careers, ensuring their observations are rooted in physical evidence rather than human emotion.

Module 2.1: Sampling Methodologies

Students engage in systems thinking by comparing Focal and Scan sampling techniques. This module provides a simulation-based learning environment where students must decide which data collection strategy best serves a specific research question, mirroring the decision-making processes of field ecologists.

Module 2.2: Ethogram & Hypothesis

Prior to the field study, students function as principal investigators by designing an Ethogram‚Äîa standardized tool for behavioral coding. This process demands precise categorization and collaborative agreement, culminating in the formulation of testable hypotheses regarding animal behavior in semi-natural environments.

Module 3.1: Field Lab - Calibration & Safety

Transitioning to the field environment, students practice professional safety protocols and synchronize timing devices to ensure inter-observer reliability. This module emphasizes the discipline required for real-world laboratory science, where precision in timing and safety compliance is paramount.

Module 3.2: Field Lab - Ruminant Herd Scan

Students apply Scan Sampling to large herds at Fossil Rim, managing complex data points in a fast-paced environment. This high-rigor activity requires students to analyze group dynamics and energy flow, providing a hands-on application of population biology concepts.

Module 3.3: Field Lab - Mega-Vertebrate Focal Study

Focusing on individual mega-fauna like Rhinos or Giraffes, students execute continuous focal sampling. This deep-dive observation challenges students to maintain concentration and record nuanced behavioral changes, simulating the stamina and attention to detail required in veterinary and ethological research.

Module 3.4: Field Lab - Habitat Engineering Audit

Shifting from biology to civil and structural engineering, students audit the physical infrastructure of the wildlife preserve. They analyze the mechanical forces and material science behind containment systems, connecting environmental science to the built world.

Module 4.1: Data Calculation

This module reinforces quantitative literacy by requiring students to convert raw field tallies into statistical summaries. Students apply mathematical formulas to calculate behavioral frequencies and means, transforming qualitative field notes into quantitative evidence for their research findings.

Module 4.2: Data Visualization

Utilizing industry-standard spreadsheet software, students translate their calculations into professional activity budget charts. This technical skill-building module emphasizes the importance of data visualization in communicating scientific results and supporting or rejecting their original hypotheses.

Module 5.1: Engineering - Problem Identification

Students transition into the role of animal welfare officers by using their data to identify biological stressors. This module requires high-level critical thinking to correlate behavioral trends (such as pacing or lethargy) with specific environmental deficiencies, setting the stage for the engineering design challenge.

Module 5.2: Engineering - Prototyping

Applying the Engineering Design Process, students collaborate to build scale models of enrichment devices. This hands-on prototyping phase encourages creativity and structural problem-solving, as students must design solutions that are biologically appropriate and mechanically sound.

Module 5.3: Engineering - Presentation

The curriculum concludes with a formal presentation where students pitch their engineering solutions to a mock committee. This module develops professional communication and public speaking skills, requiring students to justify their design decisions using the data collected during their field investigations.
